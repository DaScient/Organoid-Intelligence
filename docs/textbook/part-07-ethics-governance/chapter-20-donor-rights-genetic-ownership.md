## Opening Vignette: The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks

On January 29, 1951, a young African American woman named Henrietta Lacks walked into the colored ward of Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore, Maryland, complaining of a "knot" in her womb. She was thirty years old, the mother of five children, and had no idea that the tumor growing inside her cervix would transform the course of modern biomedical science — or that it would ignite a decades-long debate about who owns human tissue, who profits from biological discoveries, and what rights donors have over cells taken from their bodies.

Dr. Howard Jones examined Lacks, took a biopsy of the aggressive cervical tumor, and sent a sample — without her knowledge or explicit consent — to the tissue culture laboratory of Dr. George Gey. Gey had spent years trying to grow human cells outside the body, a goal that had eluded researchers for decades. Most cell samples died within days. But Lacks's cells were different. They multiplied with extraordinary vigor, doubling every twenty to twenty-four hours, thriving in conditions that killed other cultures. Gey labeled them "HeLa" — the first two letters of Henrietta's first and last names — and began distributing them to researchers around the world (Skloot, 2010).

Henrietta Lacks died on October 4, 1951, at the age of thirty-one. Her cells did not. HeLa became the first immortal human cell line, and over the following seven decades, it was used in more than 75,000 scientific publications. HeLa cells were essential to developing the polio vaccine, understanding cancer biology, mapping the human genome, studying the effects of radiation and toxic substances, and pioneering in vitro fertilization. The commercial value of products and discoveries derived from HeLa cells is estimated in the billions of dollars. The Lacks family received nothing — not a dollar in royalties, not a word of acknowledgment — for decades. Many family members could not even afford health insurance (Skloot, 2010; Beskow, 2016).

The HeLa story is not merely a historical curiosity. It is a parable — a foundational narrative about the ethical fault lines that run through every biological research enterprise that begins with human tissue. When a researcher takes cells from a donor's body, cultures them, transforms them into induced pluripotent stem cells, and differentiates those iPSCs into brain organoids capable of performing computations (see Chapter 4), a chain of transformations occurs that raises profound questions: At what point, if any, does the donor lose a legitimate claim to the material? Who owns the organoid — the donor, the researcher, the institution, the funder, or the public? What was the donor told, and what should the donor have been told? If the organoid generates intellectual property — a novel computation, a patentable architecture, a therapeutic insight — who shares in the benefits?

These questions are not new, but the advent of organoid intelligence gives them unprecedented urgency. Traditional bioethics frameworks were designed for scenarios in which tissue was used as a research tool — a means to an end. In organoid intelligence, the tissue *is* the computing substrate. The donor's genetic material is not merely a reagent; it is the medium through which intelligence is instantiated. This chapter examines the legal, ethical, and practical dimensions of donor rights and genetic ownership in the age of biological computing.

## 20.1 The Historical Context: From HeLa to iPSCs

### 20.1.1 The Henrietta Lacks Case and Its Legacy

The story of Henrietta Lacks has become, in the words of science writer Rebecca Skloot, "the most important story in the history of medical ethics you've never heard of" — a story brought to public attention by Skloot's 2010 bestseller *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks* brought it to public attention. The case illustrates several failures that remain relevant to organoid intelligence research:

1. **Absence of informed consent.** Lacks was never told that her tissue would be used for research, let alone commercialized. In 1951, there were no federal regulations requiring informed consent for tissue samples, and the concept of patient autonomy in research was not yet established in American law (Faden & Beauchamp, 1986).

2. **Racial and socioeconomic exploitation.** Lacks was a Black woman treated in a segregated ward of a hospital that served as one of the few facilities available to African Americans in Baltimore. The extraction and commercialization of her cells without consent or compensation occurred in a context of systemic racial inequality that cannot be separated from the ethical analysis (Washington, 2006).

3. **Absence of benefit-sharing.** While researchers, institutions, pharmaceutical companies, and biotechnology firms profited enormously from HeLa cells, the Lacks family lived in poverty without access to basic healthcare. This disparity crystallized the justice argument for benefit-sharing in biological research.

4. **Privacy violations.** In 2013, researchers at the European Molecular Biology Laboratory published the complete genome of a HeLa cell line without consulting the Lacks family, potentially revealing genetic predispositions and health information about living family members. The incident led to an NIH agreement granting the family some oversight over genomic data access (Callaway, 2013).

> **Key Insight:** The HeLa case demonstrates that the ethical issues surrounding donor tissue are not merely abstract philosophical questions. They involve real people, real families, and real harms. Every organoid intelligence researcher who cultures human neurons inherits the moral obligations that the scientific community failed to meet with Henrietta Lacks.

### 20.1.2 Moore v. Regents of the University of California (1990)

The most significant legal precedent for biological property rights in the United States is *Moore v. Regents of the University of California*, decided by the California Supreme Court in 1990. John Moore, a patient with hairy cell leukemia, was treated at UCLA Medical Center by Dr. David Golde. Golde recognized that Moore's T-lymphocytes had unusual properties and, without Moore's knowledge, used them to develop a patented cell line (Mo) with an estimated commercial value of over $3 billion.

When Moore discovered what had happened, he sued, arguing that he had a property interest in his own cells and that their commercial use without his consent constituted conversion (wrongful taking of property). The California Supreme Court's ruling was a landmark — and a deeply ambiguous one:

- **The court held that Moore did** ***not*** **have a property right in his excised cells.** The majority reasoned that extending property rights to human tissue would impede medical research by creating uncertainty about ownership of biological materials and would give individuals an unprecedented veto over scientific progress (Moore v. Regents, 1990).

- **However, the court held that Moore's physician had a duty to disclose his commercial interest in Moore's tissue.** The failure to disclose constituted a breach of fiduciary duty and a violation of informed consent requirements. Moore could sue for lack of informed consent, even if he could not sue for conversion.

The *Moore* decision established a legal framework that persists in much of American law: donors generally do not own their excised tissue, but they have a right to be informed about how it will be used, particularly when commercial interests are involved.

**Table 20.1: Key Legal Cases in Biological Property Rights**

| Case | Year | Jurisdiction | Key Holding | Relevance to OI |
|------|------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| *Moore v. Regents* | 1990 | California | No property right in excised cells; informed consent required | Foundational precedent for organoid donor rights |
| *Greenberg v. Miami Children's Hospital* | 2003 | Florida | No property right in donated tissue; unjust enrichment claim allowed | Implications for collaborative research models |
| *Washington University v. Catalona* | 2007 | Missouri | Researchers, not donors, control biobank samples | Challenges donor autonomy in biobank contexts |
| *Ass'n for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics* | 2013 | U.S. Supreme Court | Naturally occurring DNA not patentable; cDNA is patentable | Defines IP boundaries for biological derivatives |
| *Havasupai Tribe v. Arizona Board of Regents* | 2010 | Arizona | Tissue used beyond scope of consent; settled for $700,000 | Consent scope and Indigenous rights |
| *Henrietta Lacks Estate v. Thermo Fisher* | 2021 | Maryland | Family sued for unjust enrichment from HeLa commercialization | Ongoing implications for benefit-sharing |

### 20.1.3 The Evolution of Biological Property Rights

The legal landscape of biological property rights is fragmented and evolving. Several jurisdictions have moved beyond the *Moore* framework:

**United States.** Federal law does not provide a comprehensive framework for biological property rights. The Common Rule (45 CFR 46) governs informed consent for federally funded research but does not address ownership of tissue derivatives. The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008) protects against genetic discrimination in employment and health insurance but does not address tissue ownership. State laws vary considerably, with some states (e.g., Oregon) recognizing limited property interests in genetic information.

**European Union.** The EU's approach is grounded in the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2018) and the EU Tissues and Cells Directive (2004/23/EC). The GDPR treats genetic data as a "special category" requiring explicit consent, and the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union (Article 3) prohibits "making the human body and its parts as such a source of financial gain." This anti-commodification principle creates tension with commercial organoid research.

**United Kingdom.** The Human Tissue Act (2004) established the Human Tissue Authority (HTA) and requires "appropriate consent" for storage and use of human tissue. The UK Biobank, which holds samples from 500,000 participants, operates under a model of broad consent with governance oversight. Post-Brexit, the UK has signaled interest in developing its own regulatory framework for advanced biological materials, including organoids (Nuffield Council on Bioethics, 2017).

**Japan.** The Act on the Safety of Regenerative Medicine (2013) and the Ethical Guidelines for Medical and Biological Research Involving Human Subjects (2021) govern iPSC research. Japan's approach is notable for its emphasis on the therapeutic pipeline — from iPSC derivation to clinical application — and for the establishment of the Center for iPS Cell Research and Application (CiRA) at Kyoto University, which maintains a public iPSC bank with standardized consent protocols.

> **Key Insight:** No jurisdiction has yet developed a comprehensive legal framework specifically designed for organoid intelligence. Existing laws address tissue donation, genetic data, and intellectual property separately, but OI research involves all three simultaneously. The regulatory gap creates both risks and opportunities for the OI community.

### 20.1.4 From Tissue Donation to Computational Substrate

The transition from traditional tissue donation to organoid intelligence introduces a qualitative shift in the relationship between donor and derived material. In conventional biomedical research, donated tissue is used as an investigative tool — it is studied, experimented upon, and eventually discarded. In organoid intelligence, the tissue becomes a *functional computing system* that may persist for months or years, generate novel outputs (computations, pattern recognitions, adaptive behaviors), and produce intellectual property.

This shift has several implications for donor rights:

- **Temporal extension.** Traditional tissue donations are typically consumed in discrete experiments. OI organoids may operate continuously, raising questions about the duration and scope of consent.
- **Functional transformation.** The donor's cells are not merely preserved but fundamentally transformed — differentiated into neurons, assembled into three-dimensional architectures, integrated with electronic interfaces (Chapter 10), and potentially merged with organoids from other donors (Chapter 5). The resulting system is qualitatively different from the donated cells.
- **Output generation.** OI organoids produce computational outputs — classifications, predictions, adaptive responses — that may have commercial value. Traditional consent frameworks do not address whether donors have claims to outputs generated by systems derived from their tissue.
- **Identity persistence.** Each organoid retains the complete genome of its donor, including potentially identifiable genetic information. Unlike anonymized survey data or de-identified tissue samples, organoids carry an irreducible connection to a specific individual (see Section 20.5).

## 20.2 Informed Consent in the Age of Organoid Intelligence

### 20.2.1 Traditional Consent Models and Their Limitations

Informed consent is the ethical and legal bedrock of human subjects research. The Nuremberg Code (1947), the Declaration of Helsinki (1964, most recently revised 2013), and the Belmont Report (1979) established the principle that research participants must give voluntary, informed consent based on adequate understanding of the research's nature, purpose, risks, and benefits.

Traditional informed consent models operate under several assumptions that OI research disrupts:

1. **Specific purpose.** Traditional consent assumes that the researcher can describe the specific purpose for which the tissue will be used. OI research involves open-ended exploration — an organoid used for pattern recognition today might be repurposed for drug screening tomorrow.

2. **Foreseeable risks.** Consent requires disclosure of foreseeable risks. But the risks of OI participation — including genetic privacy breaches, unforeseen commercial applications, and the moral status implications of creating a computing system from one's cells (Chapter 19) — are difficult to enumerate in advance.

3. **Defined duration.** Traditional consent specifies a research timeline. OI organoids can persist indefinitely, and long-term studies may continue years beyond the initial consent event.

4. **Binary choice.** Traditional consent is a one-time decision: the participant consents or does not. OI research involves a continuum of activities — cell donation, iPSC derivation, organoid culture, computational use, commercialization, data sharing — each with different risk profiles and ethical implications.

> **Key Insight:** The traditional model of one-time, specific informed consent is insufficient for organoid intelligence research. The open-ended, evolving, and multi-purpose nature of OI requires new consent architectures that are dynamic, layered, and responsive to changing circumstances.

### 20.2.2 Dynamic Consent for Evolving Research

The limitations of traditional consent have led bioethicists and governance scholars to propose **dynamic consent** — a framework in which consent is an ongoing process rather than a single event (Kaye et al., 2015). Dynamic consent systems typically involve:

- **Digital platforms** that allow participants to review and update their consent preferences over time.
- **Tiered options** that let participants consent to some uses (e.g., basic research) while declining others (e.g., commercial applications, military research).
- **Real-time notification** when new uses for their tissue or data are proposed.
- **Withdrawal mechanisms** that allow participants to revoke consent for future uses while acknowledging that some past uses cannot be undone.

The UK Biobank's model offers a partial template. Participants provide broad consent at enrollment but receive regular updates about research activities, and the Biobank's Ethics and Governance Council provides oversight. However, the UK Biobank model was designed for epidemiological research, not for the creation of autonomous computing systems from donor tissue, and significant adaptation would be required for OI contexts.

**Table 20.2: Consent Models for Biological Research**

| Model | Description | Advantages | Limitations | OI Applicability |
|-------|-------------|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Specific consent** | Consent for a defined study | Clear, precise | Inflexible; requires re-consent for new uses | Low — OI involves evolving use cases |
| **Broad consent** | Consent for categories of research | Flexible; reduces re-consent burden | May not be truly "informed"; power asymmetry | Moderate — if categories are well-defined |
| **Tiered consent** | Multiple consent levels (basic, commercial, etc.) | Respects autonomy; granular control | Complex to implement; decision fatigue | High — matches OI's multi-use nature |
| **Dynamic consent** | Ongoing, updateable consent via digital platform | Responsive; empowers participants | Requires infrastructure; engagement fatigue | High — but requires sustained platform |
| **Meta-consent** | Consent about how future consent will be managed | Respects autonomy at structural level | Abstract; may not satisfy legal requirements | Moderate — useful as supplementary model |
| **Community consent** | Collective group consent (e.g., Indigenous communities) | Respects group rights; culturally appropriate | May override individual preferences | Relevant for specific populations (20.6) |

### 20.2.3 What Must Be Disclosed: Computational Use and Commercialization

For organoid intelligence research, informed consent must address disclosures that go far beyond traditional biomedical research. At minimum, a comprehensive OI consent process should disclose:

1. **The nature of organoid intelligence.** Donors must understand that their cells will be used to create a biological computing system — not simply studied as a tissue sample. This requires explaining, in accessible language, what brain organoids are, how they are created from iPSCs (Chapter 4), and what "computing" means in this context.

2. **Computational applications.** Donors should be informed about the types of computations their organoids may perform: pattern recognition, drug screening, environmental sensing, or other applications (Chapters 13–18). The open-ended nature of these applications should be acknowledged.

3. **Commercial potential.** If the research has commercial applications — or if the institution has commercial partnerships — this must be disclosed. The *Moore* ruling established that physicians and researchers have a fiduciary duty to disclose commercial interests, and this principle extends to OI research.

4. **Data generation and sharing.** OI organoids generate vast amounts of electrophysiological, genomic, and computational data. Donors should understand what data will be generated, who will have access to it, and how it will be stored, shared, and protected.

5. **Duration and persistence.** Donors should understand that organoids can persist for months or years and that their tissue may be used in ongoing experiments long after the initial donation.

6. **Moral status implications.** Given the discussion in Chapter 19, donors should be informed that their organoids may develop properties — spontaneous neural activity, oscillatory patterns, adaptive behavior — that raise questions about moral status. While no current organoid is believed to be conscious, the possibility cannot be entirely excluded, and donors deserve to know this.

7. **Withdrawal and destruction.** Donors should understand their right to withdraw consent and the practical implications of withdrawal — including whether existing organoids will be destroyed, whether data already generated will be retained, and whether withdrawal affects organoids that have been shared with other laboratories.

### 20.2.4 Consent for Future, Unforeseen Uses

One of the most challenging aspects of OI consent is addressing uses that cannot be foreseen at the time of donation. A donor who consents to having their cells used in basic neuroscience research in 2026 cannot anticipate the applications that may emerge by 2036 — autonomous biological robots, neuromorphic computing platforms, brain-computer interfaces, or applications not yet imagined.

Several approaches have been proposed:

**Broad consent with governance oversight.** The donor provides consent for a broad category of research (e.g., "organoid intelligence and related applications") while an independent ethics board reviews specific projects. This model, used by the UK Biobank and proposed by the U.S. National Academies (2023), balances flexibility with accountability but places significant trust in governance structures.

**Sunset clauses.** Consent expires after a defined period (e.g., five or ten years), after which re-consent is required for continued use. This ensures that consent remains reasonably contemporaneous with use but creates administrative burdens and risks disrupting ongoing research.

**Category-based exclusions.** Donors specify categories of use they wish to exclude (e.g., military applications, commercial products, genetic modification) rather than specifying permitted uses. This approach respects autonomy while accommodating unpredictable research directions.

> **Key Insight:** No consent model perfectly resolves the tension between donor autonomy and research flexibility. The most promising approach combines broad initial consent with robust governance oversight, transparent communication, and meaningful withdrawal rights — a framework we term **"governed broad consent."**

### 20.2.5 Model Consent Framework for OI Research

Drawing on the analysis above, we propose a model consent framework for organoid intelligence research that incorporates the following elements:

**Core Disclosures:**
- Nature of organoid intelligence and the donor's role
- Types of research and computational applications
- Commercial interests and potential revenue
- Data generation, storage, and sharing practices
- Duration of organoid use and persistence of tissue
- Moral status considerations
- Withdrawal rights and procedures

**Tiered Consent Options:**

| Tier | Activities Covered | Default |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| **Tier 1: Basic Research** | Non-commercial academic research; fundamental neuroscience | Opt-in required |
| **Tier 2: Applied Research** | Drug screening, diagnostic development, computational R&D | Opt-in required |
| **Tier 3: Commercialization** | Patent applications, licensing, commercial products | Separate opt-in |
| **Tier 4: Data Sharing** | Sharing of genomic, electrophysiological, and computational data | Granular options |
| **Tier 5: Secondary Uses** | Transfer to other institutions, biobank deposit, international sharing | Separate opt-in |

**Ongoing Engagement:**
- Annual updates on research activities and findings
- Notification of new uses or significant changes in research direction
- Digital platform for reviewing and updating consent preferences
- Access to an ombudsperson or donor advocate for questions and concerns

## 20.3 Genetic Ownership and Intellectual Property

### 20.3.1 Who Owns the Organoid?

The question of organoid ownership is deceptively simple: who owns a brain organoid derived from a specific donor's iPSCs? The answer depends on jurisdiction, institutional policy, and the specific legal theory invoked, but several claimants may assert ownership:

1. **The donor.** The cells originated in the donor's body and carry the donor's genome. Under a labor theory of property (Locke, 1689) or a bodily autonomy framework, the donor has a prima facie claim to material derived from their body. However, as *Moore v. Regents* established, this claim has limited legal support in the United States.

2. **The researcher.** The researcher invested intellectual effort and technical skill in reprogramming donor cells into iPSCs, differentiating them into neural tissue, and assembling them into functional organoids. Under intellectual property law, the researcher's creative contribution may establish inventorship or authorship.

3. **The institution.** Most research institutions require employees to assign intellectual property to the institution. University IP policies typically vest ownership of inventions and discoveries in the university, with revenue-sharing arrangements for inventors. The institution's claim is contractual rather than inherent.

4. **The funder.** Government agencies (NIH, NSF, ERC) and private foundations that fund OI research may impose conditions on IP ownership and licensing. The Bayh-Dole Act (1980) allows U.S. universities to retain title to inventions made with federal funding, subject to government march-in rights.

5. **The public.** A growing movement argues that discoveries derived from human tissue — particularly tissue donated altruistically — should be treated as public goods. Open-source biology advocates (see Section 20.3.5) contend that privatizing organoid-derived inventions violates principles of justice and impedes scientific progress.

> **Key Insight:** Organoid ownership is not a binary question with a single correct answer. Multiple stakeholders have legitimate, overlapping claims rooted in different legal and ethical traditions. A mature OI governance framework must acknowledge and balance these competing claims rather than resolving them in favor of any single party.

### 20.3.2 Patent Law Applied to Biological Derivatives

Patent law provides one framework for allocating intellectual property rights in OI research, but its application to biological materials is complex and contested.

To obtain a patent in the United States, an invention must satisfy four requirements: (1) patentable subject matter (35 U.S.C. § 101), (2) novelty (§ 102), (3) non-obviousness (§ 103), and (4) adequate disclosure (§ 112). For organoid-related inventions, the most contentious requirement is subject matter eligibility.

**Products of nature.** Under the "products of nature" doctrine, naturally occurring substances are not patentable. A researcher cannot patent a naturally occurring gene sequence, a wild-type protein, or an unmodified cell. However, modifications of natural products — including engineered cell lines, genetically modified organisms, and novel culture methods — may be patentable if they are sufficiently distinct from their natural counterparts.

**Process patents.** Even when the product itself is not patentable, the *process* of creating it may be. A novel method for differentiating iPSCs into cortical organoids, a new protocol for integrating organoids with microelectrode arrays, or an innovative technique for vascularizing organoid tissue (Chapter 5) could each be the subject of a process patent.

**Composition of matter.** A brain organoid that has been engineered with specific architectural features, genetic modifications, or functional properties not found in nature could potentially be patented as a composition of matter. The question of whether organoids are patentable compositions has not been definitively resolved in any jurisdiction.

### 20.3.3 The Myriad Genetics Ruling and Its Implications

The U.S. Supreme Court's 2013 decision in *Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, Inc.* is the most significant recent ruling on the patentability of biological materials. Myriad Genetics held patents on the BRCA1 and BRCA2 gene sequences, which are associated with hereditary breast and ovarian cancer. The Court held:

- **Naturally occurring DNA sequences are not patentable** because they are products of nature, even when isolated from the human body. Myriad did not "create" the BRCA genes; it merely discovered their location and sequence.
- **Complementary DNA (cDNA) is patentable** because it is synthetically produced and does not occur naturally. The act of creating cDNA from mRNA involves human intervention that produces a molecule distinct from anything found in nature.

The *Myriad* decision has significant implications for organoid intellectual property:

| Material | Patent Eligibility Post-Myriad | Reasoning |
|----------|-------------------------------|-----------|
| Donor's genomic DNA | Not patentable | Product of nature |
| iPSCs derived from donor cells | Potentially patentable | Process of reprogramming involves human intervention |
| Brain organoid (unmodified) | Uncertain | Derived from natural cells but assembled through human effort |
| Genetically engineered organoid | Likely patentable | Modified composition with non-natural properties |
| OI computational output | Potentially patentable (as method/process) | Novel functional application |
| Culture medium formulation | Patentable | Novel composition of matter |

> **Key Insight:** The *Myriad* framework creates a spectrum of patentability for organoid-related materials, with naturally occurring genetic material at one end (not patentable) and highly engineered biological constructs at the other (likely patentable). The organoid itself occupies an uncertain middle ground that will likely require additional judicial or legislative clarification.

### 20.3.4 Trade Secrets vs. Patents in OI Research

Not all intellectual property protection requires patents. Trade secret law offers an alternative mechanism that may be particularly relevant to OI research:

**Patents** provide strong, time-limited protection (typically 20 years from filing) but require public disclosure of the invention. Patent holders must describe their invention in sufficient detail to enable a person skilled in the art to reproduce it. This disclosure requirement promotes knowledge sharing but also enables competitors to design around the patent.

**Trade secrets** provide potentially unlimited protection (as long as the secret is maintained) but require no public disclosure. Trade secrets are protected by contractual obligations (non-disclosure agreements) and misappropriation laws. However, if a competitor independently discovers the secret or reverse-engineers the product, no legal claim can be made.

For OI research, the choice between patents and trade secrets has significant implications:

- **Culture protocols.** Detailed protocols for creating high-quality brain organoids — including specific growth factor concentrations, timing, and environmental conditions — may be more effectively protected as trade secrets than as patents, particularly given the difficulty of enforcing process patents across international borders.
- **Training paradigms.** The specific methods used to train organoids for computational tasks (Chapter 12) are likely to be highly proprietary. If training a biological neural network to perform a specific computation requires months of iterative optimization, the resulting protocol represents a significant competitive advantage.
- **Donor-specific properties.** If organoids derived from specific donors exhibit superior computational performance due to genetic factors, this knowledge has commercial value but raises ethical questions about commodifying donor-specific traits.

### 20.3.5 Open-Source Biology Models

In contrast to proprietary approaches, a growing movement advocates for open-source models in biological research, inspired by the success of open-source software. Several arguments support an open-source approach to OI:

1. **Justice.** If organoids are derived from donated human tissue, privatizing the resulting intellectual property may violate the spirit of the donation. Many donors give tissue altruistically, with the expectation that it will benefit humanity rather than enrich individual researchers or corporations.

2. **Scientific progress.** Open sharing of protocols, data, and biological materials accelerates scientific progress by enabling replication, validation, and iterative improvement. The Human Genome Project's decision to make sequence data publicly available, rather than allowing commercial sequencing companies to patent gene sequences, is a paradigmatic example (Collins et al., 2003).

3. **Equity.** Proprietary OI technologies risk creating a two-tier system in which wealthy institutions and nations have access to advanced biological computing while others are excluded. Open-source models promote global equity in access to OI technology.

Existing models for open-source biology include:

- **The BioBricks Foundation**, which maintains a registry of standardized biological parts available under open-source licenses.
- **The Open Source Drug Discovery (OSDD) initiative**, which applies open-source principles to pharmaceutical research.
- **Addgene**, a nonprofit plasmid repository that shares genetic materials under material transfer agreements.
- **The CiRA iPSC Stock Project** at Kyoto University, which provides standardized iPSC lines to qualified researchers under controlled access agreements.

> **Key Insight:** The tension between proprietary and open-source approaches to OI intellectual property reflects a deeper tension in biomedical research between incentivizing innovation through exclusivity and promoting access through openness. A hybrid model — in which foundational tools and protocols are open while specific applications are protectable — may offer the best balance.

## 20.4 Benefit-Sharing and Compensation Models

### 20.4.1 The Justice Argument for Benefit-Sharing

The principle of benefit-sharing holds that when research generates significant benefits — financial, therapeutic, or scientific — those benefits should be shared with the individuals and communities who contributed to the research. This principle is grounded in multiple ethical traditions:

- **Rawlsian justice.** John Rawls's difference principle holds that inequalities are justified only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society. If organoid research generates vast profits while donors receive nothing, the resulting inequality fails the Rawlsian test (Rawls, 1971).

- **Reciprocity.** The principle of reciprocity holds that those who contribute to a joint enterprise deserve a share of its fruits. Donors contribute an essential input — their biological material — without which the research could not proceed. Reciprocity demands some form of return (Becker, 1986).

- **Exploitation prevention.** Without benefit-sharing, the donor-researcher relationship risks becoming exploitative: the researcher extracts value from the donor's contribution while the donor bears risks (genetic privacy, psychological distress) without compensation. Benefit-sharing transforms the relationship from extractive to collaborative.

- **The Kantian imperative.** Using donor tissue solely as a means to the researcher's ends — without regard for the donor's interests — violates Kant's categorical imperative to treat persons as ends in themselves, never merely as means.

### 20.4.2 Existing Frameworks for Benefit-Sharing

Several international frameworks provide templates for benefit-sharing in biological research:

**The Nagoya Protocol (2010).** Adopted under the Convention on Biological Diversity, the Nagoya Protocol establishes a framework for access to genetic resources and the fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from their utilization. While primarily designed for biodiversity and traditional knowledge, the Protocol's principles — prior informed consent, mutually agreed terms, and benefit-sharing — are directly relevant to OI donor relations (Buck & Hamilton, 2011).

**The Human Genome Organisation (HUGO) Ethics Committee Statement on Benefit-Sharing (2000).** HUGO recommended that companies devoting a percentage (1–3%) of net profits from genetic research to healthcare infrastructure in developing countries and communities contributing to genetic research. This community-level benefit-sharing model is relevant to situations where individual donors are difficult to identify or where the research draws on population-level genetic data.

**The Common Heritage of Mankind Doctrine.** Some scholars argue that the human genome — and by extension, organoids derived from human cells — should be treated as part of the common heritage of humankind, analogous to the deep seabed or outer space. Under this doctrine, no individual or corporation can claim exclusive ownership; instead, benefits must be shared internationally (Knoppers, 1999).

### 20.4.3 Proposed Compensation Models for OI Donors

Several models for compensating OI donors have been proposed, each with different implications for justice, autonomy, and practical feasibility:

**Table 20.3: Compensation Models for OI Donors**

| Model | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| **No compensation** | Tissue is donated altruistically; no payment | Simple; avoids commodification of body | Unjust if research generates profits; perpetuates HeLa-type exploitation |
| **One-time payment** | Fixed payment at time of donation | Simple; compensates for inconvenience | Does not reflect value of downstream discoveries; may be coercive for low-income donors |
| **Royalty sharing** | Donor receives percentage of revenue from derived IP | Proportional to value; ongoing relationship | Complex to administer; raises commodification concerns; valuation disputes |
| **Trust fund** | Revenue deposited in trust for donors and their families | Protects against exploitation; long-term security | Administrative costs; governance challenges; who serves as trustee? |
| **Community benefit** | Benefits directed to donor's community (healthcare, education) | Addresses structural inequities; collective benefit | May not reflect individual donor's preferences; paternalistic |
| **Equity stake** | Donor receives equity in company commercializing organoid | Aligns interests; potential for significant returns | Complex; regulatory challenges; unsuitable for public research |
| **In-kind benefits** | Free healthcare, priority access to therapies | Directly addresses health disparities | Limited scope; difficult to value; may create perverse incentives |

### 20.4.4 Revenue Sharing and Royalty Structures

If a royalty-based compensation model is adopted, several design choices must be made:

**Royalty base.** Should royalties be calculated on gross revenue, net revenue, or profits? Gross revenue is easier to calculate but may be unfair if costs are high; net revenue or profits better reflect the actual value generated but are subject to accounting manipulation.

**Royalty rate.** What percentage is fair? The HUGO recommendation of 1–3% of net profits provides a starting point, but the appropriate rate may depend on the donor's contribution relative to other inputs (researcher effort, institutional resources, public funding). Rates in analogous industries (e.g., pharmaceutical royalties to universities) typically range from 1% to 5% of net sales.

**Duration.** Should royalties continue for the life of the patent (typically 20 years), for the life of the donor, or in perpetuity? Perpetual royalties would benefit the donor's descendants but create long-term administrative burdens.

**Allocation.** If multiple donors contribute to a single OI system — for example, if an organoid computing platform uses cells from dozens of donors — how should royalties be allocated? Equal sharing may be unfair if some donors' cells contribute more to the system's performance; contribution-based allocation requires metrics for assessing individual cell line contributions.

> **Key Insight:** Benefit-sharing is not merely an ethical aspiration — it is a practical necessity for sustainable OI research. Without fair compensation models, donor recruitment will suffer, public trust will erode, and the OI field will be vulnerable to the same legitimacy crises that have plagued genomics and biobanking.

### 20.4.5 The Anti-Commodification Objection

Not all ethicists support compensation for tissue donors. The **anti-commodification** argument holds that paying for human biological materials degrades human dignity by treating the body as a commodity. This argument has deep roots in both secular and religious ethics:

- The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Article 3) explicitly prohibits "making the human body and its parts as such a source of financial gain."
- The World Health Organization's Guiding Principles on Human Cell, Tissue and Organ Transplantation oppose commercialization of human biological materials.
- Philosopher Michael Sandel (2012) argues that market valuation of human tissue "corrupts" the goods of bodily integrity and altruistic donation by subjecting them to market logic.

The anti-commodification argument must be weighed against the justice argument: if researchers and institutions profit from donor tissue while donors receive nothing, the result is not the elimination of commodification but the one-sided commodification of the donor's body for others' benefit. A middle path — compensation that respects dignity while preventing exploitation — may be achievable through community benefit models or trust fund structures.

## 20.5 Privacy, Genetic Data, and Re-identification

### 20.5.1 Genetic Privacy Concerns with iPSC-Derived Organoids

Every iPSC-derived organoid carries the complete genome of its donor. This means that any organoid used in research, shared between laboratories, or deposited in a biobank contains information that could, in principle, reveal the donor's identity, ancestry, health predispositions, and familial relationships. The genetic privacy implications of OI research are therefore more severe than those of most biomedical research, where tissue samples are typically consumed in analysis or stored under strict access controls.

Several factors exacerbate genetic privacy concerns in OI:

1. **Persistence.** Unlike tissue samples used in destructive assays, organoids persist for extended periods. A functioning OI system may contain donor cells for months or years, during which time the genetic material remains accessible.

2. **Distribution.** Organoids may be shared between laboratories, shipped internationally, or deposited in biobanks. Each transfer increases the number of individuals and institutions with access to the donor's genetic material.

3. **Data generation.** OI systems generate vast quantities of data — electrophysiological recordings, transcriptomic profiles, computational outputs — that may contain genetic information or be linkable to genetic information through computational analysis.

4. **Advancing technology.** The cost and difficulty of whole-genome sequencing continue to decline. What was prohibitively expensive in 2010 is routine in 2026, and future technological advances may make it even easier to extract and analyze genetic information from organoid material.

### 20.5.2 Re-identification Risks from Genomic Data

The assumption that de-identification protects donor privacy has been challenged by research demonstrating that individuals can be re-identified from supposedly anonymous genomic data:

- **Gymrek et al. (2013)** demonstrated that individuals could be identified from "anonymous" DNA data by cross-referencing Y-chromosome short tandem repeat (STR) profiles with genealogy databases and public records. This approach identified personal surnames from research datasets.
- **Erlich and Narayanan (2014)** showed that even partial genomic information could be linked to identified individuals through kinship inference, surname prediction, and triangulation with public databases.
- **Sweeney et al. (2013)** found that 87% of the U.S. population could be uniquely identified using only zip code, date of birth, and sex — demographic variables often included in research datasets alongside genetic data.
- **Edge and Coop (2017)** demonstrated that relatives of research participants could be identified through genetic genealogy databases, even when the participants themselves were de-identified, creating what they termed "familial re-identification."

These findings have profound implications for OI research: any organoid that contains intact genomic DNA — which is to say, every organoid — is a potential vector for re-identification.

> **Key Insight:** De-identification of organoid-associated data provides weaker privacy protection than commonly assumed. As genomic databases grow and analytical techniques improve, the risk of re-identification increases. OI researchers must adopt privacy-by-design principles that go beyond traditional de-identification.

### 20.5.3 Regulatory Frameworks for Genetic Data Protection

Several regulatory frameworks address genetic data privacy, but none was designed specifically for the OI context:

**GDPR (EU).** The General Data Protection Regulation classifies genetic data as a "special category" of personal data requiring explicit consent for processing (Article 9). Data subjects have rights of access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), and portability. The GDPR applies extraterritorially to any organization processing data of EU residents, creating compliance challenges for international OI collaborations.

**HIPAA (US).** The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act protects "protected health information" (PHI) but has significant limitations for genetic data. HIPAA's Safe Harbor method of de-identification requires removal of 18 categories of identifiers but does not address the genomic re-identification risks described above. Moreover, HIPAA does not apply to research conducted without federal funding or outside the healthcare system.

**GINA (US).** The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act prohibits discrimination based on genetic information in health insurance and employment but does not address research use of genetic data, tissue ownership, or privacy breaches.

**Table 20.4: Comparison of Genetic Data Protection Frameworks**

| Feature | GDPR (EU) | HIPAA (US) | GINA (US) | APPI (Japan) | POPI (South Africa) |
|---------|-----------|------------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Genetic data as special category | Yes | No (part of PHI) | Specific protection | Yes | Yes |
| Explicit consent required | Yes | Authorization required | N/A | Consent required | Consent required |
| Right to erasure | Yes | Limited | No | Yes | Yes |
| Extraterritorial application | Yes | No | No | Limited | Limited |

| Penalties for breach | Up to €20M or 4% revenue | Up to $1.5M per violation | Civil penalties | Administrative orders | Up to ZAR 10M |

### 20.5.4 De-identification Strategies and Their Limitations

Several strategies have been proposed to protect donor privacy in OI research, each with limitations:

**Coding.** Replacing identifiers with a code, with the key held by a trusted intermediary. This protects against casual identification but is vulnerable to breaches of the code key and does not address genomic re-identification.

**K-anonymity.** Ensuring that any combination of quasi-identifiers (age, sex, location) maps to at least k individuals. Effective for tabular data but difficult to apply to genomic or electrophysiological data with high dimensionality.

**Differential privacy.** Adding calibrated noise to data outputs to prevent inference about individual records. Promising for aggregate statistics but may be impractical for raw electrophysiological recordings where noise degrades data quality.

**Synthetic data.** Generating artificial datasets that preserve statistical properties of the original data without containing information about any real individual. Useful for training algorithms but does not address the privacy risks inherent in the physical organoid itself.

**Federated learning.** Keeping data at source institutions and sharing only model parameters rather than raw data. Relevant to collaborative OI research but does not address the fact that the organoid itself — a physical object containing the donor's genome — may be shared between laboratories.

> **Key Insight:** No single de-identification strategy is sufficient for OI research. A defense-in-depth approach — combining multiple technical, organizational, and legal safeguards — is necessary to protect donor privacy while enabling scientific progress.

## 20.6 Special Populations and Vulnerable Donors

### 20.6.1 Pediatric Donors and Proxy Consent

Children cannot provide legally valid informed consent; instead, parents or legal guardians provide **proxy consent** (also called **permission**) on their behalf, supplemented by the child's **assent** when developmentally appropriate (typically ages 7–14). Pediatric tissue donation for OI research raises several specific concerns:

- **Long-term implications.** An organoid created from a child's cells may persist for decades, generating data and intellectual property long after the child reaches adulthood. Should the now-adult donor have the right to reconsider consent given on their behalf as a minor?
- **Genetic privacy.** Children cannot assess the privacy implications of genomic exposure. Decisions made by parents on behalf of minor donors may have consequences that the child, as an adult, would not have accepted.
- **Best interests standard.** Proxy consent must be guided by the child's best interests. If the research offers no direct therapeutic benefit to the child, the justification for donation must rely on altruistic or solidarity-based arguments — arguments that the child may not share.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (2010) recommends that pediatric research participation be limited to studies with minimal risk or a prospect of direct benefit. Whether OI research meets this threshold depends on the specific study design and the nature of the risks involved.

### 20.6.2 Deceased Donors and Posthumous Use

Tissue from deceased individuals is an important source for OI research, particularly when rare genetic conditions or neural architectures are of interest. Posthumous tissue use raises distinct ethical questions:

- **Prior consent.** Did the deceased individual consent to tissue use during their lifetime? If so, does that consent cover OI applications that may not have existed when consent was given?
- **Family authority.** In the absence of prior consent, can family members authorize tissue use? Legal frameworks vary: the U.S. Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (2006) allows designated individuals to authorize donation; the UK Human Tissue Act (2004) establishes a hierarchy of qualifying relationships.
- **Dignity interests.** Even after death, individuals may have dignity interests that constrain how their tissue is used. Using a deceased person's brain tissue to create a computing system raises questions about posthumous dignity that existing frameworks do not clearly address (Smolensky, 2009).

### 20.6.3 Indigenous Communities and Collective Consent

The *Havasupai Tribe v. Arizona Board of Regents* case (settled 2010) is a cautionary tale for OI researchers working with Indigenous communities. Members of the Havasupai tribe donated blood samples for diabetes research at Arizona State University. Without additional consent, researchers used the samples for studies on schizophrenia, inbreeding, and human migration — topics that conflicted with the tribe's cultural beliefs and identity narratives. The tribe sued and received a $700,000 settlement (Harmon, 2010).

Indigenous communities raise several specific consent challenges:

1. **Collective consent.** Many Indigenous communities understand consent as a collective, communal process rather than an individual decision. Western bioethics' emphasis on individual autonomy may conflict with communal decision-making structures.

2. **Data sovereignty.** Indigenous data sovereignty movements (e.g., the CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance) assert that Indigenous peoples have rights over the collection, ownership, and application of data about them and their communities (Carroll et al., 2020).

3. **Cultural sensitivities.** The creation of brain organoids from Indigenous tissue may raise cultural and spiritual concerns that are not captured by standard consent forms. Some communities may object to the creation of "living" neural tissue from their members' cells on cultural or spiritual grounds.

4. **Benefit-sharing.** The Nagoya Protocol's provisions for access and benefit-sharing with Indigenous and local communities provide a partial framework, but adaptation for OI contexts is needed.

### 20.6.4 Populations with Neurological Conditions

Individuals with neurological conditions — Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, autism spectrum disorders — are particularly valuable as OI donors because their cells carry genetic variants relevant to disease modeling and therapeutic development (see Chapter 16). However, these populations raise specific ethical concerns:

- **Decision-making capacity.** Some neurological conditions impair cognitive function and decision-making capacity, raising questions about the validity of consent. Careful capacity assessment is required, and surrogate consent may be necessary for individuals who lack capacity.
- **Therapeutic misconception.** Donors with serious illnesses may overestimate the likelihood that OI research will lead to treatments for their condition. Clear communication about the speculative and long-term nature of OI research is essential to prevent therapeutic misconception.
- **Double vulnerability.** Individuals with neurological conditions may face both cognitive vulnerabilities (impaired decision-making) and situational vulnerabilities (desperation for treatment), creating compounded risks of exploitation.

> **Key Insight:** Vulnerable populations require enhanced protections — not exclusion from research, but additional safeguards that ensure consent is truly voluntary and informed. Excluding vulnerable populations from OI research altogether would be paternalistic and would deprive these communities of potential research benefits.

### 20.6.5 Commercial Biobanking and Consent Fatigue

The rise of commercial biobanks — for-profit entities that collect, store, and distribute human biological materials — introduces additional concerns. Companies like 23andMe, Ancestry, and commercial tissue banks collect samples under broad consent terms that may not adequately address OI applications. Key issues include:

- **Terms of service as consent.** Commercial biobanks often obtain consent through click-through terms of service that few participants read carefully. Whether such consent is truly "informed" is debatable (Hazel & Slobogin, 2018).
- **Secondary sale.** Commercial biobanks may sell tissue samples or derived data to third parties — including OI researchers — without specific donor consent for OI applications. The consent provided at the time of sample collection may not have contemplated such uses.
- **Consent fatigue.** In an era of ubiquitous data collection, individuals are routinely asked to consent to complex terms they cannot fully evaluate. This "consent fatigue" undermines the meaningfulness of consent, particularly for novel applications like OI that most people have never heard of.

## 20.7 Toward a Comprehensive Donor Rights Framework

### 20.7.1 Principles for Ethical Organoid Sourcing

Drawing on the analysis in this chapter, we propose the following principles for ethical sourcing of human tissue for organoid intelligence research:

1. **Transparency.** Donors must receive clear, accessible, and complete information about the nature of OI research, the uses to which their tissue will be put, and the commercial interests involved. Transparency is the foundation of trust between donors and researchers.

2. **Autonomy.** Donors must have genuine choice — the ability to consent to some uses while declining others, to withdraw consent for future uses, and to receive updated information as research evolves. Autonomy requires tiered, dynamic consent mechanisms.

3. **Justice.** The benefits of OI research must be shared fairly with donors and their communities. Compensation models should reflect the donor's contribution, respect human dignity, and prevent exploitation.

4. **Privacy.** Donors' genetic information must be protected through robust technical, organizational, and legal safeguards. Privacy-by-design principles should be embedded in OI research protocols from the outset.

5. **Respect.** Donors are not merely tissue sources but participants in a collaborative enterprise. They deserve ongoing communication, acknowledgment, and the opportunity to participate in governance of the research enterprise.

6. **Inclusion.** OI research must not exclude vulnerable populations but must provide enhanced protections to ensure that consent is meaningful and that participation is truly voluntary.

7. **Accountability.** Institutions conducting OI research must be accountable for their tissue sourcing practices. Independent ethics review, public reporting, and donor advocacy mechanisms are essential.

### 20.7.2 Model Policy Recommendations

Based on these principles, we recommend the following policies for institutions conducting OI research:

**For Research Institutions:**
- Establish OI-specific ethics review protocols that address the unique features of organoid intelligence research.
- Implement dynamic consent platforms that allow donors to review and update their preferences.
- Create donor advocacy positions (ombudspersons) to represent donor interests within the institution.
- Develop benefit-sharing policies that provide fair compensation to donors when research generates commercial value.
- Conduct regular audits of tissue sourcing practices and consent procedures.

**For Funding Agencies:**
- Require evidence of ethical tissue sourcing as a condition of funding.
- Fund research on consent models, benefit-sharing mechanisms, and privacy-preserving technologies for OI.
- Support the development of international standards for OI donor rights.

**For Professional Societies:**
- Develop and maintain ethical guidelines specific to OI research.
- Provide training and certification programs for researchers working with human-derived organoids.
- Establish reporting mechanisms for ethical violations in OI research.

### 20.7.3 The Role of Biobanks and Intermediaries

Biobanks serve as intermediaries between donors and researchers, collecting, storing, and distributing human biological materials. In the OI context, biobanks play a critical role in managing consent, protecting privacy, and ensuring ethical use of donor tissue. We recommend that biobanks handling OI-related materials adopt the following practices:

- **OI-specific consent tiers.** Biobank consent forms should include explicit tiers for organoid intelligence applications, distinguishing OI research from other uses of biological materials.
- **Access controls.** Distribution of biobank materials for OI research should be subject to review by an ethics advisory board, with particular attention to the potential for genetic privacy breaches and commercial exploitation.
- **Provenance tracking.** Biobanks should maintain detailed records of the provenance of all biological materials, including the consent terms under which they were collected, the identity of the donor (in coded form), and the history of their distribution and use.
- **Donor engagement.** Biobanks should provide mechanisms for ongoing donor engagement, including notification of new uses, annual reports on research activities, and opportunities for donors to update their consent preferences.

### 20.7.4 International Harmonization Challenges

The globalization of OI research creates significant challenges for donor rights governance. An organoid derived from cells donated in Japan may be cultured in a laboratory in Germany, integrated into a computing platform in the United States, and generate data that is analyzed in Singapore. Each jurisdiction has different laws governing consent, privacy, intellectual property, and benefit-sharing, and harmonizing these frameworks is a daunting task.

Several approaches to international harmonization have been proposed:

- **Treaty-based harmonization.** An international treaty on organoid donor rights, modeled on the Nagoya Protocol or the Helsinki Declaration, could establish minimum standards for consent, privacy, and benefit-sharing. However, treaty negotiations are slow, and enforcement is often weak.
- **Mutual recognition.** Jurisdictions could agree to recognize each other's consent and privacy standards, allowing tissue to flow across borders while maintaining local governance. This approach requires trust between regulatory authorities and some degree of convergence in standards.
- **Standard-setting organizations.** International bodies (WHO, UNESCO, OECD) could develop non-binding standards and best practices for OI donor rights, which jurisdictions could then adopt voluntarily. This approach is faster than treaty negotiation but lacks enforcement mechanisms.
- **Industry self-regulation.** The OI research community could develop its own ethical standards through professional societies and industry associations. Self-regulation is flexible and responsive but may lack credibility if perceived as self-serving.

> **Key Insight:** International harmonization of OI donor rights is both essential and extraordinarily difficult. The most realistic near-term approach is a combination of standard-setting by international organizations and mutual recognition agreements between major research jurisdictions, supplemented by industry self-regulation and capacity building in developing nations.

## Code Exercise 20.1: Informed Consent Completeness Analyzer

The following Python exercise implements a tool for evaluating informed consent documents against a set of required disclosure elements for OI research. The analyzer scores consent forms based on the presence or absence of key elements identified in Section 20.2.

```python
"""
Informed Consent Completeness Analyzer for Organoid Intelligence Research.

This tool evaluates consent documents against a comprehensive checklist
of required disclosures for OI research, producing a completeness score
and identifying gaps.
"""

from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Optional


@dataclass
class DisclosureElement:
    """Represents a required disclosure element in an OI consent document."""
    category: str
    element: str
    description: str
    required: bool = True
    present: bool = False
    notes: str = ""


@dataclass
class ConsentAnalysis:
    """Results of analyzing an OI consent document."""
    document_name: str
    elements: list = field(default_factory=list)

    @property
    def total_required(self) -> int:
        return sum(1 for e in self.elements if e.required)

    @property
    def total_present(self) -> int:
        return sum(1 for e in self.elements if e.required and e.present)

    @property
    def completeness_score(self) -> float:
        if self.total_required == 0:
            return 0.0
        return self.total_present / self.total_required

    @property
    def missing_elements(self) -> list:
        return [e for e in self.elements if e.required and not e.present]

    @property
    def optional_present(self) -> list:
        return [e for e in self.elements if not e.required and e.present]


def build_oi_disclosure_checklist() -> list:
    """
    Build the comprehensive disclosure checklist for OI consent documents.
    Based on the framework proposed in Section 20.2.5.
    """
    elements = [
        # Nature of OI Research
        DisclosureElement(
            "Nature of Research", "Organoid description",
            "Explains what brain organoids are and how they are created"
        ),
        DisclosureElement(

        ),

        DisclosureElement(

        ),

        ),

        ),
        DisclosureElement(

        ),
        # Privacy and Data
        DisclosureElement(
            "Privacy", "Genetic privacy risks",
            "Explains that organoids contain donor's complete genome"
        ),
        DisclosureElement(

        DisclosureElement(
            "Privacy", "Data sharing practices",

        # Donor Rights
        DisclosureElement(

            required=False
        ),
        DisclosureElement(
            "Ethics", "Ethics review",
            "Confirms ethics board review and approval"
        ),
        DisclosureElement(
            "Ethics", "Donor advocacy",
            "Describes donor advocacy or ombudsperson resources",
            required=False
        ),
    ]
    return elements


def analyze_consent_document(
    document_name: str,
    present_elements: list,
    notes: Optional[dict] = None
) -> ConsentAnalysis:
    """
    Analyze a consent document by checking which disclosure elements
    are present.

    Args:
        document_name: Name/identifier of the consent document.
        present_elements: List of element names found in the document.
        notes: Optional dict mapping element names to reviewer notes.

    Returns:
        ConsentAnalysis with completeness score and gap analysis.
    """
    checklist = build_oi_disclosure_checklist()
    notes = notes or {}

    for element in checklist:
        if element.element in present_elements:
            element.present = True
        if element.element in notes:
            element.notes = notes[element.element]

    return ConsentAnalysis(document_name=document_name, elements=checklist)


def print_analysis_report(analysis: ConsentAnalysis) -> None:
    """Print a formatted analysis report."""
    print(f"\n{'='*65}")
    print(f"  CONSENT DOCUMENT ANALYSIS: {analysis.document_name}")
    print(f"{'='*65}")
    print(f"\n  Completeness Score: {analysis.completeness_score:.1%}")
    print(f"  Required elements present: "
          f"{analysis.total_present}/{analysis.total_required}")
    print(f"  Optional elements present: "
          f"{len(analysis.optional_present)}/{sum(1 for e in analysis.elements if not e.required)}")

    # Group by category
    categories = {}
    for elem in analysis.elements:
        categories.setdefault(elem.category, []).append(elem)

    print(f"\n{'-'*65}")
    print("  DETAILED RESULTS BY CATEGORY")
    print(f"{'-'*65}")

    for category, elements in categories.items():
        cat_present = sum(1 for e in elements if e.present)
        cat_total = len(elements)
        print(f"\n  [{category}] — {cat_present}/{cat_total}")
        for elem in elements:
            status = "✓" if elem.present else "✗"
            req = " (optional)" if not elem.required else ""
            print(f"    {status} {elem.element}{req}")
            if elem.notes:
                print(f"      Note: {elem.notes}")

    if analysis.missing_elements:
        print(f"\n{'-'*65}")
        print("  GAPS REQUIRING ATTENTION")
        print(f"{'-'*65}")
        for elem in analysis.missing_elements:
            print(f"    ✗ [{elem.category}] {elem.element}")
            print(f"      → {elem.description}")

    print(f"\n{'='*65}\n")
```

## Code Exercise 20.2: Benefit-Sharing Royalty Calculator

This exercise implements a computational model for calculating benefit-sharing royalties under various compensation frameworks discussed in Section 20.4. The model supports multiple allocation strategies for multi-donor OI platforms.

```python
"""
Benefit-Sharing Royalty Calculator for Organoid Intelligence Platforms.

Models various compensation frameworks for OI donors, including
flat-rate, proportional, and contribution-weighted royalty structures.
Supports multi-donor platforms with configurable allocation strategies.
"""

from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from enum import Enum
from typing import Optional


class AllocationStrategy(Enum):
    """Strategy for allocating royalties among multiple donors."""
    EQUAL = "equal"
    PROPORTIONAL_CELLS = "proportional_to_cell_count"
    PERFORMANCE_WEIGHTED = "performance_weighted"
    HYBRID = "hybrid"


@dataclass
class Donor:
    """Represents a tissue donor contributing to an OI platform."""
    donor_id: str
    consent_tier: int  # 1-5 per Table in Section 20.2.5
    cell_count: int  # Number of cells contributed
    performance_score: float = 1.0  # Relative contribution (0-1)
    opted_into_commercial: bool = False

    def __post_init__(self):
        if not 1 <= self.consent_tier <= 5:
            raise ValueError("Consent tier must be between 1 and 5")
        if not 0.0 <= self.performance_score <= 1.0:
            raise ValueError("Performance score must be between 0.0 and 1.0")


@dataclass
class RevenueEvent:
    """Represents a revenue-generating event from an OI platform."""
    event_id: str
    description: str
    gross_revenue: float
    costs: float = 0.0
    event_type: str = "license"  # license, patent, product, service

    @property
    def net_revenue(self) -> float:
        return max(0.0, self.gross_revenue - self.costs)


@dataclass
class RoyaltyConfig:
    """Configuration for royalty calculations."""
    base_rate: float = 0.02  # 2% default (HUGO recommendation: 1-3%)
    allocation_strategy: AllocationStrategy = AllocationStrategy.EQUAL
    use_net_revenue: bool = True  # True = net, False = gross
    minimum_payout: float = 100.0  # Minimum payout threshold
    cap_per_donor: Optional[float] = None  # Maximum annual per donor
    community_fund_rate: float = 0.005  # 0.5% to community fund
    hybrid_weights: dict = field(
        default_factory=lambda: {"equal": 0.5, "performance": 0.5}
    )


@dataclass
class RoyaltyResult:
    """Results of royalty calculation for a single donor."""
    donor_id: str
    gross_royalty: float
    community_contribution: float
    net_royalty: float
    meets_minimum: bool
    capped: bool = False
    notes: str = ""


def calculate_royalties(
    donors: list,
    events: list,
    config: RoyaltyConfig,
) -> dict:
    """
    Calculate royalties for all eligible donors based on revenue events.

    Args:
        donors: List of Donor objects.
        events: List of RevenueEvent objects.
        config: RoyaltyConfig specifying calculation parameters.

    Returns:
        Dictionary with donor results and summary statistics.
    """
    # Filter to commercially opted-in donors
    eligible = [d for d in donors if d.opted_into_commercial]

    if not eligible:
        return {
            "results": [],
            "total_revenue": sum(
                e.net_revenue if config.use_net_revenue
                else e.gross_revenue for e in events
            ),
            "total_royalties": 0.0,
            "total_community_fund": 0.0,
            "notes": "No donors opted into commercial benefit-sharing.",
        }

    # Calculate total revenue base
    revenue_base = sum(
        e.net_revenue if config.use_net_revenue
        else e.gross_revenue for e in events
    )

    # Total royalty pool
    total_royalty_pool = revenue_base * config.base_rate
    total_community_fund = revenue_base * config.community_fund_rate

    # Allocate among eligible donors
    allocations = _allocate(eligible, config)

    results = []
    total_paid = 0.0

    for donor in eligible:
        share = allocations.get(donor.donor_id, 0.0)
        gross = total_royalty_pool * share
        community = total_community_fund * share
        net = gross - community

        capped = False
        if config.cap_per_donor and net > config.cap_per_donor:
            net = config.cap_per_donor
            capped = True

        meets_min = net >= config.minimum_payout

        result = RoyaltyResult(
            donor_id=donor.donor_id,
            gross_royalty=round(gross, 2),
            community_contribution=round(community, 2),
            net_royalty=round(net, 2),
            meets_minimum=meets_min,
            capped=capped,
        )
        results.append(result)
        total_paid += net if meets_min else 0.0

    return {
        "results": results,
        "total_revenue": round(revenue_base, 2),
        "total_royalty_pool": round(total_royalty_pool, 2),
        "total_community_fund": round(total_community_fund, 2),
        "total_paid": round(total_paid, 2),
        "strategy": config.allocation_strategy.value,
    }


def _allocate(donors: list, config: RoyaltyConfig) -> dict:
    """Calculate allocation shares for each donor."""
    n = len(donors)
    if n == 0:
        return {}

    if config.allocation_strategy == AllocationStrategy.EQUAL:
        return {d.donor_id: 1.0 / n for d in donors}

    elif config.allocation_strategy == AllocationStrategy.PROPORTIONAL_CELLS:
        total_cells = sum(d.cell_count for d in donors)
        if total_cells == 0:
            return {d.donor_id: 1.0 / n for d in donors}
        return {d.donor_id: d.cell_count / total_cells for d in donors}

    elif config.allocation_strategy == AllocationStrategy.PERFORMANCE_WEIGHTED:
        total_perf = sum(d.performance_score for d in donors)
        if total_perf == 0:
            return {d.donor_id: 1.0 / n for d in donors}
        return {d.donor_id: d.performance_score / total_perf for d in donors}

    elif config.allocation_strategy == AllocationStrategy.HYBRID:
        w_eq = config.hybrid_weights.get("equal", 0.5)
        w_perf = config.hybrid_weights.get("performance", 0.5)
        total_perf = sum(d.performance_score for d in donors)
        allocations = {}
        for d in donors:
            eq_share = 1.0 / n
            perf_share = (
                d.performance_score / total_perf if total_perf > 0
                else 1.0 / n
            )
            allocations[d.donor_id] = w_eq * eq_share + w_perf * perf_share
        return allocations

    return {d.donor_id: 1.0 / n for d in donors}


def print_royalty_report(results: dict) -> None:
    """Print a formatted royalty report."""
    print(f"\n{'='*65}")
    print("  OI PLATFORM BENEFIT-SHARING REPORT")
    print(f"{'='*65}")
    print(f"  Allocation Strategy: {results['strategy']}")
    print(f"  Total Revenue Base:  ${results['total_revenue']:,.2f}")
    print(f"  Royalty Pool:        ${results['total_royalty_pool']:,.2f}")
    print(f"  Community Fund:      ${results['total_community_fund']:,.2f}")
    print(f"  Total Paid:          ${results['total_paid']:,.2f}")

    print(f"\n{'-'*65}")
    print(f"  {'Donor':<12} {'Gross':>10} {'Community':>12} "
          f"{'Net':>10} {'Status':<15}")
    print(f"  {'-'*10:<12} {'-'*10:>10} {'-'*10:>12} "
          f"{'-'*10:>10} {'-'*13:<15}")

    for r in results["results"]:
        status = "✓ Eligible" if r.meets_minimum else "Below minimum"
        if r.capped:
            status = "✓ Capped"
        print(f"  {r.donor_id:<12} ${r.gross_royalty:>9,.2f} "
              f"${r.community_contribution:>10,.2f} "
              f"${r.net_royalty:>9,.2f} {status:<15}")

    print(f"\n{'='*65}\n")
```

## Discussion Questions

1. **The HeLa legacy.** Henrietta Lacks's cells were taken without her consent and commercialized without her family's knowledge. If a similar situation occurred today with organoid intelligence — a donor's cells being used to create a commercially valuable computing system without adequate consent — what legal and ethical remedies should be available? How should the OI community ensure that the mistakes of the HeLa era are not repeated?

2. **Dynamic consent and practicality.** Dynamic consent frameworks (Section 20.2.2) promise to give donors ongoing control over how their tissue is used. But dynamic consent requires sustained donor engagement, digital infrastructure, and administrative resources. Is dynamic consent practically feasible for large-scale OI research programs, or is it an aspirational ideal? What level of engagement can researchers reasonably expect from donors over periods of years or decades?

3. **The ownership puzzle.** Section 20.3.1 identifies at least five potential claimants to organoid ownership: the donor, the researcher, the institution, the funder, and the public. Is it possible to construct a coherent legal framework that fairly balances all five claims? Or must some claims take precedence over others? Which, and why?

4. **Benefit-sharing and commodification.** The anti-commodification argument (Section 20.4.5) holds that paying donors for tissue degrades human dignity. The justice argument holds that *not* paying donors while others profit from their tissue is exploitative. How should the OI community navigate this tension? Is there a form of compensation that respects dignity while preventing exploitation?

5. **Genetic privacy in practice.** Given the re-identification risks described in Section 20.5.2, is it possible to conduct OI research while meaningfully protecting donor genetic privacy? What technical and organizational measures would be required? At what point, if any, should privacy concerns override the benefits of OI research?

6. **Indigenous data sovereignty.** The Havasupai case (Section 20.6.3) illustrates the risks of using Indigenous tissue without culturally appropriate consent. How should OI researchers engage with Indigenous communities? Should collective consent supplement or replace individual consent? What role should Indigenous governance structures play in OI research decisions?

7. **Posthumous organoids.** If brain organoids are created from a deceased person's cells, what rights, if any, do the deceased person's family members have over the resulting organoid? Should posthumous consent requirements be different for OI research (where the tissue becomes a computing system) than for traditional biomedical research? How do concepts of posthumous dignity apply?

8. **International harmonization.** OI research is global, but donor rights frameworks vary dramatically across jurisdictions (Section 20.7.4). Should the OI community pursue binding international treaties, voluntary standards, or market-driven harmonization? What are the advantages and risks of each approach for donors in developing nations?

## Further Reading

### Legal Foundations
- **Moore v. Regents of the University of California, 51 Cal.3d 120 (1990).**
  *The foundational U.S. case on property rights in excised human tissue. Essential reading for understanding the legal framework governing donor rights.*

- **Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, Inc., 569 U.S. 576 (2013).**
  *The Supreme Court's ruling on patentability of naturally occurring DNA. Directly relevant to the IP status of organoid-derived biological materials.*

- **Greenberg v. Miami Children's Hospital Research Institute, 264 F.Supp.2d 1064 (S.D. Fla. 2003).**
  *Key case on unjust enrichment claims by tissue donors. Illustrates the limitations of property-based arguments and the potential of equitable remedies.*

### Bioethics and Informed Consent
- **Skloot, R. (2010).** *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks.* Crown Publishing.
  *The definitive account of the HeLa case and its implications for bioethics. Accessible, compelling, and essential background for anyone working with human tissue.*

- **Faden, R., & Beauchamp, T. (1986).** *A History and Theory of Informed Consent.* Oxford University Press.
  *The standard reference on informed consent theory and practice. Provides the conceptual foundations for the consent frameworks discussed in this chapter.*

- **Kaye, J., Whitley, E. A., Lund, D., Morrison, M., Teare, H., & Melham, K. (2015).** "Dynamic consent: a patient interface for twenty-first century research networks." *European Journal of Human Genetics*, 23(2), 141–146.
  *The seminal paper on dynamic consent frameworks. Proposes the digital consent platforms discussed in Section 20.2.2.*

### Genetic Privacy and Data Protection
- **Gymrek, M., McGuire, A. L., Golan, D., Halperin, E., & Erlich, Y. (2013).** "Identifying personal genomes by surname inference." *Science*, 339(6117), 321–324.
  *Demonstrates the feasibility of re-identifying individuals from anonymous genetic data. Foundational for understanding privacy risks in OI research.*

- **Erlich, Y., & Narayanan, A. (2014).** "Routes for breaching and protecting genetic privacy." *Nature Reviews Genetics*, 15(6), 409–421.
  *Comprehensive review of genetic privacy threats and countermeasures. Essential reading for OI researchers handling genomic data.*

### Benefit-Sharing and Justice
- **Knoppers, B. M. (1999).** "Status, sale and patenting of human genetic material: an international survey." *Nature Genetics*, 22(1), 23–26.
  *International survey of legal frameworks for human genetic material. Useful for understanding cross-jurisdictional variation in donor rights.*

- **Buck, M., & Hamilton, C. (2011).** "The Nagoya Protocol on Access to Genetic Resources and the Fair and Equitable Sharing of Benefits Arising from their Utilization to the Convention on Biological Diversity." *Review of European Community & International Environmental Law*, 20(1), 47–61.
  *Analysis of the Nagoya Protocol's benefit-sharing framework and its potential application to human biological materials.*

### Indigenous Rights and Collective Consent
- **Carroll, S. R., Garba, I., Figueroa-Rodríguez, O. L., Holbrook, J., Lovett, R., Materechera, S., ... & Hudson, M. (2020).** "The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance." *Data Science Journal*, 19(1), 43.
  *Establishes the CARE framework (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics) for Indigenous data governance. Critical for OI research involving Indigenous communities.*

- **Harmon, A. (2010).** "Indian Tribe Wins Fight to Limit Research of Its DNA." *The New York Times*, April 21, 2010.
  *Reporting on the Havasupai case, which set important precedents for consent scope and Indigenous rights in genetic research.*

### Organoid Ethics
- **Hyun, I., Scharf-Deering, J. C., & Bhatt, J. (2020).** "Ethical issues related to brain organoid research." *Brain Research*, 1732, 146653.
  *Focused analysis of ethical challenges specific to brain organoid research, including donor consent and moral status questions.*

- **Nuffield Council on Bioethics. (2017).** *Human Embryo Culture: Discussions Concerning the Statutory Time Limit for Maintaining Human Embryos in Culture in the Light of Recent Scientific Developments.* London: Nuffield Council on Bioethics.
  *UK policy analysis relevant to the governance of human-derived biological materials in research settings.*

## Future Directions

### 🔮 Open Problems

1. **Consent for emergent computational outputs.** Current consent frameworks address the use of tissue for defined research purposes. But OI organoids may generate emergent computational outputs — novel classifications, unexpected pattern recognitions, spontaneous problem-solving behaviors — that were not and could not have been foreseen at the time of consent. How should consent frameworks be structured to address outputs that are genuinely unpredictable? Does the donor have any claim to intellectual property arising from emergent computation performed by tissue derived from their cells?

2. **Cross-donor organoid platforms.** Advanced OI systems may integrate organoids from multiple donors into a single computing platform (Chapter 5 discusses multi-organoid assembloids). When a multi-donor system generates intellectual property, how should ownership and benefits be allocated among donors whose cells contribute differently to the system's function? What consent frameworks can manage the complexity of multi-donor contributions to a single computing substrate?

3. **Donor identity and organoid identity.** Each organoid carries its donor's complete genome, creating an irreducible link between donor identity and organoid identity. As organoids develop complex computational behaviors (Chapter 12), does the organoid acquire a form of "identity" that is related to but distinct from the donor's? What are the philosophical and legal implications of this relationship between donor identity and organoid function?

4. **Regulatory arbitrage in OI tissue sourcing.** Differences in donor protection laws across jurisdictions create the potential for regulatory arbitrage — sourcing tissue from countries with weaker protections to avoid the requirements of jurisdictions with stronger ones. How can the international community prevent a "race to the bottom" in OI donor protections while respecting national sovereignty and avoiding paternalism toward developing nations?

5. **Artificial gametes and synthetic genomes.** Emerging technologies for creating artificial gametes and synthetic genomes may eventually enable the creation of organoids from entirely synthetic genetic material, bypassing the need for human donors altogether. If this becomes feasible, does it eliminate the donor rights problem — or does it create new ethical concerns about the creation of novel human-like genetic material? How do frameworks of ownership apply to organisms derived from non-existent persons?

## Chapter Summary

This chapter examined the ethical, legal, and practical dimensions of donor rights and genetic ownership in the age of organoid intelligence. Beginning with the foundational case of Henrietta Lacks and the landmark *Moore v. Regents* decision (Section 20.1), we traced the evolution of biological property rights from a legal landscape that gave donors virtually no control over their excised tissue to emerging frameworks that recognize donor autonomy, privacy, and dignity as central values. The transition from traditional tissue donation to organoid intelligence — in which donor cells become functional computing substrates — introduces qualitative shifts that existing legal and ethical frameworks were not designed to address.

We analyzed informed consent in the OI context (Section 20.2), arguing that the traditional model of one-time, specific consent is insufficient for research that is open-ended, evolving, and multi-purpose. Dynamic consent, tiered consent, and governed broad consent models offer more appropriate alternatives, and we proposed a comprehensive consent framework incorporating core disclosures, tiered options, and ongoing engagement mechanisms. The intellectual property landscape (Section 20.3) is equally complex: the *Myriad Genetics* ruling defines boundaries for patenting biological materials, but the organoid itself occupies an uncertain legal position, and tensions between proprietary and open-source approaches remain unresolved.

Benefit-sharing and compensation (Section 20.4) require balancing the justice argument for fair returns to donors with the anti-commodification argument against marketizing the human body. We surveyed proposed models — royalties, trust funds, community benefits, equity stakes — and argued that sustainable OI research requires meaningful benefit-sharing to maintain public trust and donor participation. Genetic privacy (Section 20.5) presents challenges that go beyond traditional de-identification, as organoids carry complete donor genomes vulnerable to re-identification through advancing analytical techniques. Special populations (Section 20.6) — children, deceased individuals, Indigenous communities, persons with neurological conditions — require enhanced protections that respect both autonomy and vulnerability.

Finally, we proposed a comprehensive donor rights framework (Section 20.7) built on principles of transparency, autonomy, justice, privacy, respect, inclusion, and accountability, with model policy recommendations for research institutions, funding agencies, and professional societies. The challenge of international harmonization — aligning diverse legal traditions across jurisdictions — remains one of the most significant obstacles to a just and effective global governance framework for OI donor rights.

**In the next chapter**, we turn to the global governance of organoid intelligence more broadly. Chapter 21 examines the international regulatory landscape for OI technology, the role of multilateral institutions in setting standards, and the challenge of governing a technology that crosses national borders, disciplinary boundaries, and cultural traditions. Where this chapter focused on the rights of individual donors, Chapter 21 addresses the governance of the OI enterprise as a whole — from biosecurity concerns to technology transfer, from export controls to equitable access.
