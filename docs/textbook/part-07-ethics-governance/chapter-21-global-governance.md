## Opening Vignette: The Asilomar Moment

In February 1975, 140 biologists, physicians, and lawyers gathered at the Asilomar Conference Center on the Monterey Peninsula of California to confront a question that no scientific community had ever faced so directly: Should scientists voluntarily restrict their own research? The occasion was the advent of recombinant DNA technology — the ability to cut, splice, and recombine genetic material from different organisms — which had opened extraordinary possibilities for understanding gene function but also raised the specter of engineered pathogens, ecological disruption, and biological hazards that no one fully understood.

The road to Asilomar had begun eighteen months earlier. In June 1973, at the Gordon Research Conference on Nucleic Acids, scientists first raised public concerns about the potential risks of recombinant DNA experiments. By July 1974, a committee chaired by Paul Berg — who would later win the Nobel Prize for his pioneering work on recombinant DNA — published an extraordinary letter in *Science* and *Nature* calling for a voluntary moratorium on certain categories of recombinant DNA experiments until the risks could be assessed (Berg et al., 1974). It was, as historian Susan Wright later observed, "the first time in history that scientists had voluntarily agreed to limit their own freedom to experiment" (Wright, 1994).

The Asilomar conference itself was contentious, exhausting, and ultimately transformative. Over four days, participants debated the nature and magnitude of the risks, the adequacy of existing containment measures, and the appropriate role of government regulation. Some argued that the risks were overblown and that self-imposed restrictions would cripple a promising field. Others warned that a failure to act responsibly would invite heavy-handed government intervention that would be far more restrictive than anything the scientists might impose on themselves. The molecular biologist Sydney Brenner, who chaired the plasmid working group, argued forcefully that the scientific community had a moral obligation to demonstrate that it could govern itself (Fredrickson, 2001).

The conference produced a set of guidelines that classified recombinant DNA experiments by risk level and prescribed corresponding levels of physical and biological containment. These guidelines were subsequently adopted, with modifications, by the National Institutes of Health (NIH) as the *NIH Guidelines for Research Involving Recombinant DNA Molecules* (1976), which remain in force today in updated form. The Asilomar framework was not perfect — critics noted that it focused narrowly on laboratory biosafety while ignoring broader social, ethical, and ecological questions (Krimsky, 1982) — but it established a precedent of extraordinary significance: scientists could, and sometimes must, govern the trajectory of their own field before governments and publics forced them to do so.

Half a century later, the organoid intelligence community faces its own Asilomar moment. The technology is different — we are not splicing genes but culturing neural tissue into computational substrates — but the structural challenge is strikingly similar. A powerful new technology is advancing faster than the regulatory frameworks designed to govern it. The potential benefits are enormous: biological computers that could revolutionize drug discovery, neurological research, and artificial intelligence (see Chapter 13). The risks are real but poorly understood: questions of moral status (Chapter 19), donor rights (Chapter 20), dual-use potential, and the prospect of engineering systems that blur the boundary between biological tissue and thinking machines. And the international dimension is unavoidable: organoid intelligence research is conducted in dozens of countries under different regulatory regimes, creating the possibility of regulatory arbitrage — researchers migrating to jurisdictions with the weakest oversight.

The question this chapter addresses is whether the global community can build governance structures adequate to the challenge — structures that protect human dignity, promote responsible innovation, and prevent catastrophic misuse, while allowing the extraordinary scientific potential of organoid intelligence to be realized. The Asilomar precedent suggests that scientific self-governance is possible but insufficient. The history of nuclear governance suggests that international institutions are necessary but slow. The ongoing struggles over AI governance suggest that getting the timing right — building frameworks before the technology outpaces them — is the hardest problem of all.

## 21.1 Lessons from History: Governing Transformative Technologies

### 21.1.1 The Asilomar Conference and Recombinant DNA Governance

The Asilomar Conference of 1975, described in the opening vignette, remains the canonical example of scientific self-governance. Its legacy is instructive for the OI community, but its limitations must also be understood.

**Strengths of the Asilomar model:**

1. **Speed.** The moratorium and subsequent guidelines were developed in less than two years, far faster than any governmental regulatory process could have achieved. In a field moving as rapidly as organoid intelligence, speed of governance is a critical advantage.

2. **Technical expertise.** The guidelines were drafted by scientists who understood the technology at a level of detail that regulators could not match. The risk classifications and containment protocols were technically sophisticated and practically implementable.

3. **Legitimacy within the scientific community.** Because the guidelines were developed by scientists themselves, they enjoyed a degree of voluntary compliance that externally imposed regulations might not have achieved.

4. **Preemption of restrictive legislation.** By demonstrating responsible self-governance, the scientific community forestalled the passage of restrictive federal legislation that was being actively considered by the U.S. Congress (Fredrickson, 2001).

**Limitations of the Asilomar model:**

1. **Narrow framing.** The conference focused almost exclusively on laboratory biosafety — the risk that engineered organisms might escape and cause environmental or health damage. It did not address broader ethical questions about the direction of the technology, equitable access to its benefits, or the social implications of genetic engineering (Jasanoff, 2005).

2. **Exclusion of non-scientists.** Despite the presence of a few lawyers and journalists, the conference was overwhelmingly composed of molecular biologists. The public, patient advocacy groups, ethicists, and social scientists were largely absent. This produced guidelines that reflected scientific priorities but not necessarily societal values (Krimsky, 1982).

3. **U.S.-centric perspective.** The Asilomar framework was developed primarily by American scientists and adopted by American institutions. International coordination was limited, and many countries developed their own, sometimes divergent, regulatory approaches.

4. **Inadequacy over time.** As the technology evolved, the original risk categories became outdated, and the guidelines required repeated revision. The process of updating the guidelines was slow and contentious, revealing the difficulty of maintaining adaptive governance over decades (Wright, 1994).

> **Key Insight:** The Asilomar model demonstrates that scientific self-governance can be rapid, technically informed, and effective in the short term. However, sustainable governance of transformative technologies requires broader participation, international coordination, and adaptive mechanisms that the Asilomar framework did not fully provide. OI governance must learn from both the successes and the failures of the Asilomar precedent.

### 21.1.2 Nuclear Governance: The IAEA Model

The governance of nuclear technology offers a different model — one based on international institutions, binding treaties, and verification mechanisms. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), established in 1957, was created to promote the peaceful use of nuclear energy while preventing the proliferation of nuclear weapons. Its mandate, famously described as "Atoms for Peace," embodies the dual-use dilemma that is central to many transformative technologies, including organoid intelligence.

**Key features of the IAEA model:**

| Feature | Description | Relevance to OI |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Treaty basis** | The Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT, 1968) provides the legal foundation | OI may eventually require an international treaty or convention |
| **Safeguards system** | Material accountancy, inspections, and monitoring to verify compliance | Analogous systems for tracking organoid complexity or dual-use capabilities |
| **Technical standards** | Safety standards, security guidelines, and emergency preparedness frameworks | OI will need international technical standards for biosafety and biocontainment |
| **Capacity building** | Technical assistance to developing countries for peaceful nuclear applications | Equitable access to OI technology requires capacity building in the Global South |
| **Tiered obligations** | Different obligations for nuclear-weapon states and non-nuclear-weapon states | Tiered governance based on organoid complexity or institutional capacity |

The IAEA model is instructive but also cautionary. The agency has been criticized for being slow to adapt to new challenges, for lacking enforcement power independent of the UN Security Council, and for an inherent tension between its promotional and regulatory mandates (Goldschmidt, 1999). Its safeguards system, while sophisticated, has failed to prevent several instances of clandestine nuclear weapons development. Nevertheless, the IAEA demonstrates that international governance of a dual-use technology is feasible, and that a combination of treaties, institutions, inspections, and technical standards can create a framework — imperfect but real — for managing global risks.

### 21.1.3 AI Governance: Current Efforts and Challenges

The most immediately relevant precedent for OI governance is the ongoing effort to govern artificial intelligence. The parallels are striking: AI, like OI, is a dual-use technology with transformative potential and significant risks; it is advancing faster than regulatory frameworks; it is developed in multiple countries under different legal and cultural traditions; and it raises fundamental questions about autonomy, accountability, and the nature of intelligence.

Key developments in AI governance include:

- **The EU AI Act (2024).** The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, establishing a risk-based classification system with corresponding obligations for developers and deployers. High-risk AI systems — including those used in critical infrastructure, education, employment, and law enforcement — face stringent requirements for transparency, human oversight, and data quality (European Commission, 2021).

- **The OECD AI Principles (2019).** Adopted by 46 countries, these principles emphasize inclusive growth, human-centered values, transparency, robustness, and accountability. They are non-binding but influential in shaping national regulatory approaches.

- **The UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2021).** The first global normative instrument on AI ethics, adopted by 193 member states, establishes principles including proportionality, safety, fairness, sustainability, privacy, human oversight, and accountability.

- **The Bletchley Declaration (2023).** Signed by 28 countries at the UK AI Safety Summit, this declaration acknowledges the potential risks of frontier AI systems and commits signatories to international cooperation on AI safety.

- **U.S. Executive Order on AI Safety (2023).** Establishes requirements for safety testing, red-teaming, and reporting of dual-use foundation models, along with guidelines for federal agencies' use of AI.

> **Key Insight:** The AI governance landscape is fragmented, rapidly evolving, and characterized by a tension between the EU's regulatory approach, the U.S.'s market-oriented approach, and China's state-directed approach. OI governance will likely face similar fragmentation, and the AI governance experience suggests that early international coordination — before positions harden — is essential.

### 21.1.4 What OI Can Learn from These Precedents

Across these three historical examples — recombinant DNA, nuclear technology, and artificial intelligence — several common themes emerge that are directly relevant to organoid intelligence governance:

1. **Timing matters.** The most effective governance frameworks were established early, before the technology was widely deployed and before entrenched interests made reform difficult. The Asilomar moratorium was called before any recombinant DNA accident had occurred; the IAEA was established before nuclear technology had proliferated widely. In contrast, AI governance has struggled because the technology was already deeply embedded in commercial products and national security applications before serious regulatory efforts began.

2. **Scientific self-governance is necessary but not sufficient.** The scientific community has unique technical expertise and can move faster than governments, but it lacks the democratic legitimacy, enforcement power, and breadth of perspective needed for comprehensive governance.

3. **International coordination is essential.** Technologies that cross borders require governance frameworks that cross borders. National regulation alone creates opportunities for regulatory arbitrage and fails to address global risks.

4. **Governance must be adaptive.** Technologies evolve, risks change, and governance frameworks must be designed to evolve with them. Static regulations become obsolete; adaptive governance mechanisms — such as periodic review, sunset clauses, and tiered regulation — are more resilient.

5. **Multi-stakeholder participation improves outcomes.** Governance frameworks developed by scientists alone (Asilomar) tend to be narrowly focused; those developed by states alone (IAEA) tend to reflect geopolitical power dynamics; those developed with broad participation — scientists, ethicists, civil society, industry, and affected communities — tend to be more robust and legitimate.

**Table 21.1: Comparative Governance Models for Transformative Technologies**

| Dimension | Recombinant DNA (Asilomar) | Nuclear (IAEA/NPT) | Artificial Intelligence | Organoid Intelligence (Proposed) |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------------------|------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Primary governance actors** | Scientists | States, international organizations | Mix of states, industry, civil society | Multi-stakeholder: scientists, ethicists, regulators, public |
| **Legal basis** | Voluntary guidelines, later NIH regulations | International treaties | Mix of binding regulations and soft law | To be determined |
| **Risk classification** | Four levels based on biological hazard | Safeguards categories based on fissile material | Risk-based tiers (EU AI Act) | Tiered by organoid complexity and capability |
| **International coordination** | Limited; primarily U.S.-based | Extensive; 178 NPT signatories | Emerging; fragmented | Needed from the outset |
| **Enforcement** | Funding conditionality (NIH) | IAEA inspections; Security Council sanctions | Fines, market access restrictions | To be designed |
| **Public participation** | Minimal | Limited | Growing | Essential |
| **Adaptive mechanisms** | Periodic guideline revision | Review conferences every 5 years | Regulatory sandboxes, sunset clauses | Built-in review cycles |
| **Timeline** | Moratorium within 1 year; guidelines within 2 years | Treaty 11 years after technology; agency 12 years | Regulation 7+ years after commercial deployment | Proactive: governance before widespread deployment |

## 21.2 The Current Regulatory Landscape

### 21.2.1 ISSCR Guidelines for Stem Cell and Organoid Research

The International Society for Stem Cell Research (ISSCR) has played a central role in establishing ethical and scientific standards for stem cell research globally. The ISSCR *Guidelines for Stem Cell Research and Clinical Translation*, most recently updated in 2021, provide the most comprehensive international guidance currently applicable to organoid research.

Key provisions relevant to OI include:

- **Stem Cell Research Oversight (SCRO) committees.** The ISSCR recommends that all institutions conducting stem cell research establish specialized oversight committees to review protocols involving human embryonic stem cells, iPSCs, organoids, and chimeric organisms. SCRO committees are expected to assess scientific merit, ethical acceptability, and regulatory compliance (ISSCR, 2021).

- **Categories of research review.** The 2021 guidelines establish three categories:
  - **Category 1:** Research permissible after standard ethics review (e.g., iPSC derivation from consenting adults).
  - **Category 2:** Research requiring specialized oversight (e.g., organoid culture beyond certain complexity thresholds, chimeric embryo research).
  - **Category 3:** Research that should not be pursued at this time (e.g., human reproductive cloning, gestating chimeric embryos).

- **Organoid-specific guidance.** The 2021 guidelines explicitly address organoids for the first time, recommending that "research involving human brain organoids or assembloids should be subject to enhanced oversight" when such structures develop features that could be associated with sentience, pain perception, or consciousness (ISSCR, 2021, Recommendation 2.2.2).

> **Key Insight:** The ISSCR guidelines represent the closest thing to an international regulatory standard for organoid research, but they are non-binding recommendations. Compliance depends on institutional willingness, funder requirements, and journal publication policies. They lack enforcement mechanisms and may not keep pace with the rapid development of OI technology. The classification of complex neural organoids under Category 2 ("enhanced oversight") rather than Category 3 ("not to be pursued") reflects the current scientific consensus that organoids do not yet possess consciousness — a consensus that Chapter 19 argues may need to be revisited as organoid capabilities advance.

### 21.2.2 The 14-Day Rule and Its Relevance to Organoids

One of the most consequential governance instruments in human embryology is the "14-day rule" — the widely adopted prohibition on culturing human embryos *in vitro* beyond 14 days post-fertilization. First proposed by the UK's Warnock Committee in 1984 and subsequently adopted in legislation or guidelines in more than a dozen countries, the 14-day rule was designed as a pragmatic compromise: it allowed early embryo research (essential for understanding infertility, genetic disease, and developmental biology) while setting a clear temporal boundary beyond which the moral status of the embryo was considered too significant to permit experimentation (Warnock, 1985; Hyun et al., 2021).

The 14-day rule is directly relevant to organoid governance for two reasons:

1. **As a structural precedent.** The rule demonstrates that a simple, enforceable boundary — a bright line — can be more effective than complex, graduated standards. Its clarity is its strength: researchers know exactly when they must stop culturing an embryo, ethics committees know exactly what to enforce, and the public knows exactly what is permitted. A similar bright-line approach could be valuable for OI governance, perhaps based on organoid complexity, neural activity thresholds, or functional capabilities.

2. **As a substantive challenge.** The 14-day rule applies to embryos, not organoids, and organoids do not develop through the same ontological trajectory as embryos. Brain organoids can be cultured for months or even years without passing through an embryonic stage. The rule does not, therefore, directly regulate organoid research. However, the *reasoning* behind the rule — that moral status increases with developmental complexity — is directly applicable to organoids, particularly neural organoids that develop increasingly sophisticated neural architectures over time (Sawai et al., 2020).

The ISSCR's 2021 decision to relax the 14-day rule — recommending that research beyond 14 days be permitted with "a compelling scientific rationale, a specialized scientific and ethics oversight process, and no suitable alternative" — reflects a recognition that the rule, while valuable, had become an obstacle to important research that poses minimal ethical risk relative to the insights it could yield (ISSCR, 2021). This relaxation may presage a similar evolution in OI governance: initial bright-line rules that are gradually replaced by more nuanced, risk-based approaches as scientific understanding matures.

### 21.2.3 FDA Regulation of Organoid-Derived Products

In the United States, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has regulatory authority over products derived from organoid research, but the regulatory framework was not designed with organoids in mind. Organoid-derived products may fall under several overlapping regulatory categories:

- **Human Cells, Tissues, and Cellular and Tissue-Based Products (HCT/Ps).** Under 21 CFR Parts 1270 and 1271, the FDA regulates human tissues intended for implantation, transplantation, infusion, or transfer. Organoids derived from human cells may qualify as HCT/Ps, subjecting them to registration, listing, donor eligibility, and current good tissue practice (CGTP) requirements.

- **Biological Products.** Under the Public Health Service Act, Section 351, biological products — including cell therapies, gene therapies, and tissue-engineered products — require premarket approval through a Biologics License Application (BLA). Organoid-based therapeutic products (e.g., retinal organoids for transplantation, liver organoids for cell therapy) would likely be regulated as biological products.

- **Combination Products.** When organoids are integrated with non-biological components — such as microelectrode arrays, microfluidic chips, or electronic interfaces (see Chapter 13) — the resulting product may be classified as a "combination product" under 21 CFR Part 3, requiring coordination between the FDA's Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research (CBER) and Center for Devices and Radiological Health (CDRH).

- **Investigational New Drug (IND) Applications.** Clinical trials involving organoid-derived products require IND applications, which must demonstrate safety, manufacturing consistency, and a scientific rationale for the proposed clinical use.

The fundamental challenge is that the FDA's regulatory categories were designed for products that fit neatly into one classification: drugs, devices, or biologics. Organoid intelligence systems — particularly those that function as computational substrates rather than therapeutic products — do not fit neatly into any existing category. An OI system used for drug screening might be regulated as a laboratory tool (and thus largely exempt from FDA premarket review), while the same system used to model a patient's disease might be considered a diagnostic device, and one used for computational purposes might fall into an entirely unregulated space.

### 21.2.4 EMA and European Regulatory Approaches

The European Medicines Agency (EMA) regulates organoid-derived products under the framework for Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs), established by Regulation (EC) No 1394/2007. ATMPs encompass gene therapy, somatic cell therapy, and tissue-engineered products, and organoid-based therapies would likely be classified under the latter two categories.

Key features of the European approach include:

- **Centralized authorization.** ATMPs require marketing authorization from the EMA's Committee for Advanced Therapies (CAT), providing a single point of regulatory review for all EU member states.

- **The precautionary principle.** European regulatory culture is generally more precautionary than the U.S. approach, requiring demonstration of safety before market approval rather than relying primarily on post-market surveillance.

- **Ethical review at the member state level.** While the EMA handles scientific and regulatory review, ethical review of research involving human tissues remains the responsibility of national ethics committees in each member state, creating potential inconsistencies across the EU.

- **The EU Clinical Trials Regulation (536/2014).** This regulation harmonizes clinical trial requirements across the EU and establishes the Clinical Trials Information System (CTIS) for centralized submission and assessment. Organoid-based clinical trials must comply with these requirements.

## 21.3 Gaps in Existing Governance

### 21.3.1 The Classification Problem

Perhaps the most fundamental governance challenge for organoid intelligence is the classification problem: existing regulatory frameworks categorize biological materials into established categories — tissues, cells, organs, devices, drugs, biologics — and organoid intelligence systems do not fit neatly into any of them. This is not merely a bureaucratic inconvenience; it has profound practical consequences for which regulations apply, which agencies have jurisdiction, and what protections are afforded to research subjects and the public.

Consider the following classification dilemma. A brain organoid cultured from a patient's iPSCs, integrated with a microelectrode array, connected to a microfluidic perfusion system, and trained to perform pattern recognition is simultaneously:

- **A human tissue** (derived from human cells, containing human genetic material)
- **A medical device** (integrated with electronic components, potentially used for diagnostic purposes)
- **A biological product** (a cell-based therapy if used for transplantation)
- **A computing system** (performing information processing and adaptive computation)
- **A potential moral subject** (if it develops features associated with sentience; see Chapter 19)

No existing regulatory framework was designed to govern an entity that is all of these things simultaneously. The result is regulatory fragmentation: different aspects of the same OI system may be regulated by different agencies under different legal authorities, with gaps in coverage and potential conflicts in requirements.

### 21.3.2 Dual-Use Concerns in OI Research

The concept of "dual use" — research that is intended for beneficial purposes but could be misused for harmful ones — is well established in the governance of biological and nuclear technologies. OI research raises dual-use concerns in several specific domains:

1. **Biological weapons.** Organoid systems capable of modeling neural function could, in principle, be used to develop neurotoxins, design agents that target specific neural pathways, or test the efficacy of biological weapons against human neural tissue. While these applications are speculative, the biological weapons convention (BWC) framework does not explicitly address organoid-based research (Koblentz, 2009).

2. **Surveillance and interrogation.** OI systems designed to model cognitive processes could potentially be repurposed for developing enhanced interrogation techniques, cognitive profiling tools, or neurosurveillance technologies. The intersection of OI with brain-computer interface (BCI) technology creates additional dual-use risks.

3. **Autonomous weapons.** If OI systems are developed that can perform real-time adaptive computation, they could potentially be integrated into autonomous weapons systems — a prospect that raises the same concerns about autonomous lethal decision-making that have driven calls for regulation of lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS) (Scharre, 2018).

4. **Cognitive enhancement.** OI technology could be used for cognitive enhancement applications — improving human cognitive function through biological-digital hybrid systems. While not inherently harmful, unequal access to cognitive enhancement technologies could exacerbate social inequalities and raise questions about fairness and justice.

> **Key Insight:** Dual-use governance for OI cannot simply be grafted onto existing biosecurity frameworks. The unique features of OI — the integration of biological and electronic components, the potential for adaptive learning, and the blurring of boundaries between computation and cognition — require new thinking about what constitutes a dual-use risk and how such risks should be managed.

### 21.3.3 Cross-Border Research and Regulatory Arbitrage

Organoid intelligence research is a global enterprise, conducted by teams in the United States, Europe, Japan, China, Australia, South Korea, Singapore, and elsewhere. This internationalization creates the risk of regulatory arbitrage — researchers or companies migrating to jurisdictions with the weakest oversight to conduct research that would be restricted or prohibited elsewhere.

Historical precedents include:

- **Stem cell tourism.** Following the imposition of restrictions on federal funding for human embryonic stem cell research in the United States (2001–2009), some stem cell research migrated to jurisdictions with more permissive regulatory environments, including Singapore, the UK, and certain EU member states. Simultaneously, unregulated "stem cell clinics" proliferated in countries with limited regulatory oversight, offering unproven therapies to desperate patients (Sipp et al., 2017).

- **Reproductive tourism.** Differences in national regulations on assisted reproductive technologies — including surrogacy, preimplantation genetic testing, and sex selection — have created a global market in which patients travel to jurisdictions where their desired procedure is legal and affordable (Cohen, 2012).

- **Gene editing tourism.** Following He Jiankui's announcement in 2018 that he had created the first gene-edited human babies, concerns were raised about the inadequacy of international governance mechanisms for preventing rogue researchers from conducting prohibited experiments in permissive jurisdictions (Cyranoski, 2019).

The risk of regulatory arbitrage in OI research is heightened by several factors: the relatively low capital requirements for organoid culture (compared to, say, nuclear technology), the portability of iPSC lines and culture protocols, the ease of international collaboration through digital communication, and the absence of a specific international framework for OI governance.

### 21.3.4 The Speed Gap: Technology vs. Regulation

One of the most persistent challenges in technology governance is the "pacing problem" — the tendency of technological innovation to outpace regulatory development. This gap is particularly acute for organoid intelligence because:

- **The technology is advancing exponentially.** As documented in Chapters 6–13, OI capabilities have expanded dramatically in a short period, from simple neural spheroids to complex organoid assembloids capable of rudimentary computation, sensory response, and adaptive learning.

- **The regulatory process is inherently slow.** New legislation typically requires years of deliberation, drafting, committee review, public comment, and political negotiation. Even updating existing guidelines — such as the NIH guidelines for recombinant DNA research — can take years.

- **Uncertainty impedes regulation.** Effective regulation requires a reasonably clear understanding of the risks and benefits of the technology being regulated. When both are highly uncertain — as is the case with OI — regulators face a dilemma: regulate too early and risk stifling beneficial innovation; regulate too late and risk failing to prevent harm (Marchetti, 2012).

## 21.4 Proposed Governance Frameworks for OI

### 21.4.1 Adaptive Governance Models

Traditional regulatory models — in which legislatures enact specific rules that agencies enforce — are poorly suited to rapidly evolving technologies. Adaptive governance offers an alternative approach based on the following principles:

1. **Iterative regulation.** Rather than attempting to establish comprehensive rules at the outset, adaptive governance proceeds through cycles of provisional regulation, monitoring, evaluation, and revision. Rules are understood to be provisional and subject to updating as new information becomes available.

2. **Regulatory experimentation.** Different jurisdictions, institutions, or sectors may adopt different regulatory approaches, generating evidence about the effectiveness of alternative governance strategies. This "regulatory federalism" — analogous to U.S. Supreme Court Justice Louis Brandeis's concept of states as "laboratories of democracy" — allows learning through experimentation (Marchetti, 2012).

3. **Stakeholder feedback.** Adaptive governance incorporates formal mechanisms for ongoing input from researchers, industry, ethicists, affected communities, and the public. Governance is understood as a continuous dialogue rather than a one-time legislative act.

4. **Sunset clauses and mandatory reviews.** Regulations include built-in expiration dates or mandatory review periods, ensuring that rules are periodically reassessed in light of technological and scientific developments.

The adaptive governance model is particularly well suited to OI because the technology is evolving rapidly, the risks and benefits are poorly understood, and there is significant uncertainty about the trajectory of the field. By proceeding incrementally rather than comprehensively, adaptive governance avoids both premature overregulation and dangerous underregulation.

### 21.4.2 Tiered Regulation Based on Organoid Complexity

A promising approach to OI governance is tiered regulation — a system in which the stringency of oversight is proportional to the complexity, capability, and potential moral status of the organoid system in question. This approach draws on the risk-based classification systems used in the EU AI Act, the ISSCR guidelines, and the NIH guidelines for recombinant DNA research.

**Table 21.3: Proposed Tiered Regulatory Framework for Organoid Intelligence**

| Tier | Organoid Characteristics | Examples | Required Oversight | Governance Rationale |
|------|-------------------------|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Tier 1: Basic** | Simple organoids, limited cell diversity, no neural network formation | Intestinal organoids, liver organoids for drug screening | Standard institutional ethics review (IRB/REC) | Minimal moral status concerns; primary regulatory issues are biosafety and donor consent |
| **Tier 2: Intermediate** | Neural organoids with organized structure, basic electrical activity | Cortical organoids with layered architecture, retinal organoids | Enhanced ethics review; SCRO committee oversight; documented containment protocols | Emerging complexity raises questions about functional capacity; precautionary oversight warranted |
| **Tier 3: Advanced** | Complex neural organoids or assembloids with sustained network activity, adaptive behavior | Multi-region brain assembloids, organoids exhibiting learning or stimulus response | Specialized OI ethics board; independent external review; ongoing monitoring; mandatory reporting | Significant functional capabilities; potential moral status concerns; dual-use considerations |
| **Tier 4: Frontier** | Systems exhibiting indicators associated with sentience, consciousness, or self-awareness | Hypothetical: organoids with integrated sensory processing, evidence of subjective experience | National-level review; international notification; mandatory moratorium pending assessment | Profound moral status implications; existential governance challenge; requires societal deliberation |

> **Key Insight:** Tiered regulation allows governance to scale with technological capability. It avoids the twin pitfalls of overregulating simple organoid systems (which would impede beneficial research without meaningful ethical gain) and underregulating complex ones (which would fail to address genuine moral and safety concerns). The key challenge is defining objective, measurable criteria for classifying organoid systems into tiers — a challenge that requires close collaboration between neuroscientists, ethicists, and governance experts (see also Chapter 19, Section 19.4).

### 21.4.3 Institutional Oversight Structures

Effective governance requires institutional infrastructure — organizations with the mandate, expertise, resources, and authority to implement governance frameworks. Several institutional models have been proposed for OI governance:

1. **Dedicated OI oversight agencies.** Some scholars have proposed the creation of new national or international agencies specifically mandated to govern organoid intelligence, analogous to the IAEA for nuclear technology or the HFEA for human embryology in the UK. Such agencies would have the advantage of specialized expertise but might lack the political support and resources needed for establishment.

2. **Integration into existing agencies.** An alternative approach is to expand the mandates of existing regulatory agencies — such as the FDA, EMA, or national bioethics commissions — to include OI governance. This approach leverages existing institutional infrastructure and expertise but risks overburdening agencies that are already stretched thin.

3. **Networked governance.** A third approach involves creating networks of existing institutions — research ethics boards, national bioethics commissions, international scientific societies, and regulatory agencies — that coordinate their activities through shared standards, mutual recognition agreements, and information-sharing mechanisms. This distributed model is more flexible and adaptive than centralized governance but may suffer from coordination failures and accountability gaps.

### 21.4.4 Self-Governance by the Scientific Community

The Asilomar precedent (Section 21.1.1) suggests that scientific self-governance can play an important role in the early stages of a technology's development. Several mechanisms for OI self-governance have been proposed:

- **Professional codes of conduct.** Scientific societies such as the ISSCR, the International Brain Organoid Society (IBOS), or the IEEE could develop codes of conduct for OI researchers, specifying ethical obligations, safety standards, and best practices.

- **Voluntary moratoria.** In cases where the risks of a particular research direction are unclear, the scientific community could impose voluntary moratoria — temporary suspensions of specific research activities — while risks are assessed. The Berg letter of 1974 provides a precedent for this approach.

- **Peer review and publication standards.** Journals could adopt editorial policies requiring OI research submissions to demonstrate compliance with ethical guidelines, document informed consent processes, and disclose potential dual-use implications.

- **Open science and transparency.** Open access to data, methods, and results increases the scientific community's ability to monitor and evaluate OI research. Transparency is a necessary (though not sufficient) condition for effective self-governance.

### 21.4.5 Multi-Stakeholder Governance Models

The most robust governance frameworks for transformative technologies tend to involve multiple stakeholders — not just scientists and regulators, but also ethicists, industry representatives, civil society organizations, affected communities, and the public. Multi-stakeholder governance addresses the legitimacy deficit of expert-only governance and the knowledge deficit of government-only governance.

Successful models of multi-stakeholder governance include:

- **The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN),** which governs internet domain names through a multi-stakeholder model involving governments, the private sector, civil society, and the technical community.

- **The Codex Alimentarius Commission,** which develops international food safety standards through collaboration between governments, scientific experts, industry, and consumer organizations.

- **The Forest Stewardship Council (FSC),** which sets standards for sustainable forestry through a governance structure that gives equal weight to environmental, social, and economic stakeholders.

For OI governance, a multi-stakeholder model might include:

- **Scientific community:** researchers, scientific societies, research institutions
- **Government:** regulatory agencies, legislative bodies, national bioethics commissions
- **Industry:** biotechnology companies, pharmaceutical firms, technology companies
- **Ethics and social science:** bioethicists, philosophers, social scientists, legal scholars
- **Civil society:** patient advocacy groups, disability rights organizations, Indigenous communities
- **Public:** mechanisms for public deliberation, citizen panels, participatory governance

## 21.5 International Coordination and Agreements

### 21.5.1 The Case for an International OI Governance Body

The international dimension of OI governance is unavoidable. Organoid intelligence research is conducted globally; iPSC lines, data, and protocols cross national borders routinely; and the risks of OI — dual-use misuse, moral status violations, regulatory arbitrage — are inherently transnational. This section argues that effective OI governance requires some form of international coordination, and examines the institutional options for achieving it.

The case for an international governance body rests on several arguments:

1. **Prevention of regulatory arbitrage.** As discussed in Section 21.3.3, differences in national regulatory frameworks create incentives for researchers and companies to relocate to jurisdictions with the weakest oversight. An international governance body could establish minimum standards that all participating jurisdictions agree to enforce, reducing the scope for regulatory arbitrage.

2. **Coordination of risk assessment.** The risks of OI are complex, uncertain, and evolving. No single national government has the expertise or resources to assess these risks comprehensively. An international body could pool scientific expertise, coordinate risk assessment activities, and disseminate findings to national regulators.

3. **Harmonization of standards.** Divergent national standards for organoid characterization, quality control, safety testing, and ethical review create barriers to international collaboration and impede the translation of OI research into clinical and commercial applications. An international body could develop harmonized technical standards that facilitate cross-border research while maintaining safety and ethical protections.

4. **Legitimacy and trust.** An international governance body with broad membership and transparent procedures would enhance the legitimacy of OI governance and build public trust in the technology. This is particularly important for a technology that raises fundamental questions about human identity, consciousness, and moral status.

### 21.5.2 Existing International Frameworks That Could Be Extended

Rather than creating an entirely new international organization, OI governance could be integrated into or coordinated by existing international frameworks:

**Table 21.4: Existing International Frameworks Relevant to OI Governance**

| Organization | Mandate | Potential Role in OI Governance | Limitations |
|-------------|---------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| **UNESCO** | Education, science, culture, ethics | Develop normative frameworks; facilitate intergovernmental dialogue; extend existing bioethics declarations | Limited enforcement power; slow consensus-building |
| **WHO** | Global health | Regulate OI-derived therapeutics; coordinate safety monitoring; develop clinical guidelines | Focus on health products; limited mandate for computational OI |
| **OECD** | Economic cooperation and development | Develop policy recommendations; coordinate regulatory approaches among member states; fund OI governance research | Limited to member states (primarily wealthy nations) |
| **WTO** | International trade | Address trade barriers related to OI products; develop rules on IP and technology transfer | Narrow trade focus; limited ethical mandate |
| **UN Human Rights Council** | Human rights | Address moral status and dignity questions; develop rights-based frameworks for OI governance | Political dynamics; limited technical expertise |

| **ISSCR** | Stem cell science | Update guidelines to address OI specifically; coordinate international scientific consensus | Non-governmental; no enforcement power |

### 21.5.3 UNESCO, WHO, and OECD Roles

Among existing international organizations, three are particularly well positioned to contribute to OI governance:

**UNESCO.** The United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization has extensive experience in developing normative instruments for science and technology governance. Its *Universal Declaration on the Human Genome and Human Rights* (1997), *International Declaration on Human Genetic Data* (2003), and *Universal Declaration on Bioethics and Human Rights* (2005) established foundational principles for governing biological research at the international level. UNESCO's Intergovernmental Bioethics Committee (IGBC) and International Bioethics Committee (IBC) could serve as platforms for developing OI-specific normative frameworks, drawing on their established legitimacy and broad membership.

**WHO.** The World Health Organization's role in OI governance is most relevant to organoid-derived therapeutic products and public health applications. The WHO's Expert Advisory Committee on Developing Global Standards for Governance and Oversight of Human Genome Editing, established in 2018 following the He Jiankui affair, provides a model for how the organization could engage with OI governance — convening expert committees, developing governance frameworks, and maintaining a global registry of OI research activities analogous to the WHO Global Registry for Human Genome Editing.

**OECD.** The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development has been active in developing policy frameworks for emerging technologies, including its *Recommendation on Responsible Innovation in Neurotechnology* (2019) — the first international policy instrument specifically addressing neurotechnology governance. This recommendation, which addresses brain organoids among other neurotechnologies, establishes principles of responsible innovation, safety, consent, privacy, and agency that are directly applicable to OI governance. The OECD's Working Party on Bio-, Nano- and Converging Technologies could serve as a platform for coordinating OI regulatory approaches among member states.

### 21.5.4 Harmonization Challenges Across Legal Traditions

One of the most significant obstacles to international OI governance is the diversity of legal traditions across jurisdictions. Different countries approach the governance of biological research through fundamentally different legal and cultural lenses:

- **Common law traditions** (U.S., UK, Australia, Canada) tend to rely on case-by-case adjudication, regulatory guidance, and relatively permissive approaches that allow innovation unless specific harms are demonstrated.

- **Civil law traditions** (France, Germany, Japan, much of Latin America) tend to rely on comprehensive legislative codes and the precautionary principle, requiring demonstration of safety before permitting innovation.

- **Islamic legal traditions** may engage with OI through the lens of *maqasid al-shariah* (objectives of Islamic law), which include the preservation of life, intellect, and progeny, and which may impose specific constraints on research involving human neural tissue.

- **Confucian-influenced traditions** (China, South Korea, Japan) may emphasize collective welfare and social harmony over individual rights, leading to different approaches to informed consent, donor autonomy, and benefit-sharing.

- **Indigenous legal traditions** may prioritize communal rights, intergenerational obligations, and holistic understandings of personhood that differ fundamentally from Western bioethical frameworks (see Chapter 20, Section 20.6.3).

Harmonization across these traditions does not require uniformity; it requires mutual recognition, reciprocity, and a shared commitment to core principles — human dignity, safety, transparency, and accountability — that can be instantiated differently in different legal and cultural contexts.

### 21.5.5 Model Treaty Provisions for OI Governance

While a comprehensive international OI governance treaty is unlikely in the near term, it is useful to envision what such a treaty might include. The following model provisions draw on existing international law and the governance frameworks discussed in this chapter:

1. **Definitions.** The treaty would establish internationally agreed definitions of key terms: organoid, organoid intelligence, neural organoid, assembloid, computational substrate, and related concepts.

2. **Minimum regulatory standards.** Participating states would commit to implementing minimum regulatory standards for OI research, including institutional ethics review, informed consent, biosafety protocols, and reporting requirements.

3. **Tiered governance.** The treaty would establish a tiered governance framework (similar to Table 21.3) based on organoid complexity and capability, with increasing oversight requirements at higher tiers.

4. **Prohibition of specific applications.** The treaty would prohibit specific applications deemed incompatible with human dignity, such as the weaponization of OI systems, the creation of organoids intended to suffer, or the commercial sale of organoid-derived consciousness.

5. **International oversight body.** The treaty would establish an international oversight body with the mandate to coordinate risk assessment, develop technical standards, facilitate information sharing, and monitor compliance.

6. **Reporting and transparency.** Participating states would commit to reporting OI research activities, significant findings, and safety incidents to the international oversight body.

7. **Technology transfer and capacity building.** The treaty would include provisions for equitable technology transfer and capacity building, ensuring that the benefits of OI research are accessible to developing countries.

8. **Review and amendment.** The treaty would include provisions for periodic review and amendment, ensuring that the governance framework remains adaptive to technological change.

> **Key Insight:** The history of international treaty-making suggests that achieving even basic agreement on these provisions would be a lengthy and politically complex process. However, the experience of the Biological Weapons Convention, the UNESCO bioethics declarations, and the OECD neurotechnology recommendation demonstrates that international normative frameworks — even non-binding ones — can shape national policies and establish shared expectations. The OI community should begin the conversation about international governance now, rather than waiting for a crisis to force it.

## 21.6 Institutional Design for OI Oversight

### 21.6.1 National OI Ethics Committees

Many countries have established national bioethics commissions or committees that advise governments on ethical issues arising from advances in the life sciences. These bodies — such as the Presidential Commission for the Study of Bioethical Issues (U.S., now defunct), the Nuffield Council on Bioethics (UK), the German Ethics Council (*Deutscher Ethikrat*), and the French National Consultative Ethics Committee (*Comité Consultatif National d'Éthique*, CCNE) — could play an important role in OI governance by:

- Conducting public deliberations on the ethical implications of OI technology
- Advising governments on regulatory approaches to OI
- Developing ethical guidelines for OI research and applications
- Engaging the public in dialogue about the future of biological computing
- Reviewing specific OI research proposals that raise novel ethical questions

The effectiveness of national ethics committees depends on their independence from government, their access to scientific expertise, their engagement with diverse stakeholders, and their ability to produce timely recommendations. Committees that are understaffed, politicized, or disconnected from the research community are unlikely to provide effective governance.

### 21.6.2 Research Ethics Board (IRB/REC) Adaptations

At the institutional level, research ethics boards — known as Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) in the United States and Research Ethics Committees (RECs) in Europe — are the primary mechanism for ethical oversight of human subjects research. These boards review research protocols for ethical acceptability, assess risks and benefits, evaluate informed consent procedures, and monitor ongoing research.

However, existing IRB/REC structures were designed for research involving human participants, not research involving human-derived tissues that may have independent moral significance. Adapting these structures for OI research requires several modifications:

1. **Expanded expertise.** OI research ethics review requires expertise in neuroscience, bioengineering, philosophy of mind, and computational science — disciplines not traditionally represented on IRBs/RECs. Ethics boards reviewing OI research should include members with relevant technical expertise or access to expert consultants.

2. **Revised risk-benefit frameworks.** Traditional risk-benefit analysis focuses on risks to human research participants. OI research may involve risks to the organoid itself (if it has morally relevant properties), risks to the donor (genetic privacy, dignitary harms), and risks to society (dual-use concerns, social disruption). Ethics boards need expanded frameworks that capture these diverse risk dimensions.

3. **Ongoing monitoring.** Unlike most human subjects research, OI systems may persist and evolve over months or years. Ethics boards should implement ongoing monitoring mechanisms — including periodic re-review, mandatory milestone reporting, and the authority to modify or terminate research if unexpected developments raise new ethical concerns.

4. **Cross-institutional coordination.** Multi-center OI research projects — involving collaborators in different institutions, countries, and regulatory jurisdictions — require coordinated ethics review to ensure consistent standards. Mechanisms for mutual recognition of ethics approvals, joint review panels, and shared ethical protocols should be developed.

### 21.6.3 Industry Self-Regulation and Professional Standards

As OI technology moves from academic laboratories toward commercial applications (see Chapter 13), industry self-regulation becomes an important complement to governmental oversight. Industry self-regulation can take several forms:

- **Industry associations.** Biotechnology and pharmaceutical industry associations — such as the Biotechnology Innovation Organization (BIO), the International Consortium for Organoid Technologies, and the European Federation of Pharmaceutical Industries and Associations (EFPIA) — could develop industry-wide standards for OI safety, quality, and ethics.

- **Corporate governance.** Individual companies developing OI technology could establish internal ethics review boards, appoint chief ethics officers, and adopt corporate policies on responsible OI development. Companies such as Cortical Labs and FinalSpark have already begun to engage with ethical questions surrounding their OI research.

- **Certification and accreditation.** Third-party certification bodies could develop standards for OI research facilities, manufacturing processes, and products, providing independent verification of compliance with safety and ethical requirements.

### 21.6.4 Public Engagement and Democratic Governance

Effective governance of OI requires meaningful public engagement — not merely informing the public about OI technology, but involving citizens in deliberation about how the technology should be developed, regulated, and deployed. Public engagement serves several functions:

1. **Democratic legitimacy.** In democratic societies, decisions about transformative technologies should reflect the values and preferences of the citizenry, not just the judgments of experts. Public engagement provides democratic legitimacy for governance decisions that might otherwise be perceived as technocratic imposition.

2. **Epistemic contribution.** Diverse publics bring diverse perspectives, values, and lived experiences that enrich governance deliberation. Non-experts may identify risks, values, and social implications that experts overlook.

3. **Social license.** Public acceptance of OI technology — the "social license to operate" — depends on public trust, which in turn depends on transparency, accountability, and the perception that governance structures reflect public values.

Models for public engagement in technology governance include:

- **Citizens' assemblies and juries** — randomly selected panels of citizens who deliberate on specific questions and produce recommendations for policymakers
- **Public consultations** — structured processes for soliciting and responding to public input on proposed policies or regulations
- **Deliberative polling** — surveys that measure informed public opinion after participants have been provided with balanced information and opportunities for discussion
- **Participatory technology assessment** — collaborative processes in which experts and non-experts jointly assess the implications of new technologies

### 21.6.5 Enforcement Mechanisms and Compliance

Governance frameworks are only as effective as their enforcement mechanisms. OI governance requires a range of enforcement tools, calibrated to the severity of violations and the nature of the regulatory obligation:

| Enforcement Mechanism | Description | Appropriate For |
|----------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Funding conditionality** | Compliance with ethical and safety standards as a condition of receiving research funding | Publicly funded research; most effective for academic researchers |
| **Publication requirements** | Journal policies requiring demonstration of ethical compliance as a condition of publication | Academic research; enforced by editorial boards and peer reviewers |
| **Licensing and accreditation** | Requirement to obtain a license or accreditation before conducting OI research | Institutional oversight; effective for controlling entry into the field |
| **Regulatory sanctions** | Fines, injunctions, product recalls, and other administrative penalties for regulatory violations | Commercial applications; requires statutory authority and enforcement infrastructure |
| **Criminal liability** | Criminal prosecution for the most serious violations (e.g., creation of sentient organoids without oversight, weaponization of OI) | Deterrence of the most egregious misconduct; requires clear legal definitions |
| **Professional discipline** | Revocation of professional credentials, debarment from research funding, institutional sanctions | Individual researchers; enforced by professional societies and funding agencies |

## 21.7 Responsible Innovation and Anticipatory Governance

### 21.7.1 The Responsible Innovation Framework

Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI) is an emerging governance paradigm that seeks to align research and innovation processes and outcomes with the values, needs, and expectations of society. The RRI framework, developed primarily in the European context, emphasizes four dimensions (Stilgoe, Owen, & Macnaghten, 2013):

1. **Anticipation.** Researchers and innovators should systematically consider the potential impacts — positive and negative, intended and unintended — of their work before those impacts materialize. This requires foresight activities such as scenario planning, technology assessment, and horizon scanning.

2. **Reflexivity.** Researchers should reflect critically on their own assumptions, values, and motivations, and on the broader social and ethical context of their work. Reflexivity involves asking not just "Can we do this?" but "Should we do this?" and "For whom are we doing this?"

3. **Inclusion.** The governance of research and innovation should involve diverse stakeholders, including those who may be affected by the technology but are not part of the research community. Inclusion requires active efforts to engage marginalized voices and address power imbalances in governance processes.

4. **Responsiveness.** Governance mechanisms should be adaptive and responsive to new information, changing circumstances, and evolving public values. Responsiveness requires institutional capacity for learning, flexibility, and course correction.

Applied to organoid intelligence, the RRI framework calls for:

- Systematic anticipation of the ethical, social, and safety implications of OI research at every stage of the research process
- Reflexive engagement by OI researchers with the moral status questions raised in Chapter 19 and the donor rights issues raised in Chapter 20
- Inclusive governance structures that involve not just scientists and regulators but also ethicists, affected communities, disability rights advocates, Indigenous peoples, and the general public
- Responsive governance mechanisms that can adapt as OI technology evolves and as our understanding of organoid capabilities deepens

### 21.7.2 Technology Assessment for OI

Technology assessment (TA) is a systematic process for evaluating the potential consequences of new technologies and providing policy-relevant information to decision-makers. Several approaches to TA are relevant to OI:

- **Constructive Technology Assessment (CTA):** Developed in the Netherlands, CTA integrates societal considerations into the technology development process itself, rather than assessing technologies only after they have been developed. For OI, CTA would involve embedding ethical reflection and stakeholder engagement into the research process from the earliest stages.

- **Real-Time Technology Assessment (RTTA):** Developed at Arizona State University's Consortium for Science, Policy, and Outcomes, RTTA integrates social science research with natural science research in real time, enabling continuous assessment and adjustment as the technology evolves. The Human Practices component of the iGEM (International Genetically Engineered Machine) competition provides a model for how RTTA principles could be implemented in OI research.

- **Participatory Technology Assessment (pTA):** Developed by organizations such as the Loka Institute and the Danish Board of Technology, pTA involves non-expert citizens in technology assessment processes through consensus conferences, scenario workshops, and citizen panels. The UK's Sciencewise program and the Dutch Rathenau Institute have conducted pTAs on topics related to neuroscience and biotechnology that could be extended to OI.

### 21.7.3 Scenario Planning and Foresight Methods

Anticipatory governance requires systematic methods for envisioning possible futures and developing governance strategies that are robust across multiple scenarios. Key foresight methods applicable to OI governance include:

1. **Scenario planning.** The development of multiple plausible future scenarios — typically ranging from optimistic to pessimistic — that explore how OI technology might evolve and how different governance approaches might perform under different conditions. Scenarios should consider technological trajectories, regulatory developments, public attitudes, commercial dynamics, and geopolitical factors.

2. **Horizon scanning.** The systematic monitoring of emerging trends, signals, and developments that could affect the trajectory of OI technology or the adequacy of existing governance frameworks. Horizon scanning should cover not only scientific and technological developments but also social, political, economic, and cultural factors.

3. **Delphi methods.** Structured expert consultations in which panels of experts iteratively refine their judgments about the likelihood, timing, and impact of future developments. Delphi studies could be used to assess the timeline for achieving specific OI capabilities (e.g., organoids capable of sustained complex computation), the probability of specific risk scenarios, and the likely effectiveness of different governance approaches.

4. **Red-teaming.** The deliberate assignment of teams to identify vulnerabilities, failure modes, and unintended consequences of proposed governance frameworks. Red-teaming is widely used in military and cybersecurity contexts and could be adapted for OI governance to stress-test regulatory proposals before implementation.

### 21.7.4 Building Governance Capacity Ahead of Technological Capability

The central insight of anticipatory governance is that governance capacity must be built *before* — not after — the technology it is designed to govern reaches maturity. For OI, this means investing in governance infrastructure now, while the technology is still in its early stages and the governance design space is still open.

Specific investments in governance capacity include:

- **Training programs** for researchers, ethics board members, regulators, and policymakers on OI-specific governance challenges
- **Research funding** for governance scholarship — ethical analysis, legal research, social science investigation, and institutional design
- **Institutional development** — establishing or adapting ethics committees, regulatory bodies, and international coordination mechanisms
- **Public engagement** — building public awareness, understanding, and deliberative capacity around OI technology
- **International dialogue** — initiating conversations among governments, scientific societies, and international organizations about the governance architecture needed for OI

> **Key Insight:** The most dangerous governance failure is not overregulation or underregulation but *late* regulation — building governance capacity only after a crisis has demonstrated the inadequacy of existing frameworks. The He Jiankui affair in gene editing, the Chernobyl disaster in nuclear technology, and the Cambridge Analytica scandal in data governance all demonstrate the costs of reactive governance. The OI community has the opportunity — and the obligation — to learn from these failures and build governance capacity proactively.

                "dimension": dim.name,
                "risk_weight": risk_weights[dim.risk_level],
                "gap_fraction": len(uncovered_in) / len(jurisdictions),
                "weighted_score": round(weighted_score, 2),
            })

    # Jurisdiction-level scores
    for jur in jurisdictions:
        covered = sum(
            1 for d in dimensions if jur.covered_dimensions.get(d.name, "")
        )
        total = len(dimensions)
        report["jurisdiction_scores"][jur.jurisdiction] = {
            "covered": covered,
            "total": total,
            "coverage_pct": round(100 * covered / total, 1),
        }

    # Sort risk-weighted gaps by severity
    report["risk_weighted_gaps"].sort(
        key=lambda x: x["weighted_score"], reverse=True
    )

    return report


def print_gap_report(report: dict) -> None:
    """Print a formatted gap analysis report."""
    print("=" * 70)
    print("ORGANOID INTELLIGENCE REGULATORY GAP ANALYSIS")
    print("=" * 70)

    print("\n--- UNIVERSAL GAPS (no jurisdiction has coverage) ---")
    for gap in report["universal_gaps"]:
        print(f"\n  ⚠️  {gap['dimension']} [{gap['risk_level'].upper()}]")
        print(f"      {gap['description']}")

    print("\n--- PARTIAL GAPS ---")
    for gap in report["partial_gaps"]:
        coverage_pct = gap['coverage_ratio'] * 100
        print(f"\n  📋 {gap['dimension']} [{gap['risk_level'].upper()}]"
              f" — {coverage_pct:.0f}% coverage")
        print(f"      Uncovered in: {', '.join(gap['uncovered_in'])}")

    print("\n--- JURISDICTION COVERAGE SCORES ---")
    for jur, score in report["jurisdiction_scores"].items():
        bar_len = int(score["coverage_pct"] / 5)
        bar = "█" * bar_len + "░" * (20 - bar_len)
        print(f"  {jur:<20} {bar} {score['coverage_pct']}%"
              f" ({score['covered']}/{score['total']})")

    print("\n--- HIGHEST PRIORITY GAPS (risk-weighted) ---")
    for entry in report["risk_weighted_gaps"][:5]:
        print(f"  🔴 {entry['dimension']}: weighted score ="
              f" {entry['weighted_score']}")

    print("\n" + "=" * 70)


# Run the analysis
if __name__ == "__main__":
    jurisdictions = build_jurisdiction_profiles()
    report = analyze_gaps(jurisdictions, OI_DIMENSIONS)
    print_gap_report(report)

def assess_maturity(
    domain: GovernanceDomain,
    responses: list[bool],
) -> MaturityLevel:
    """
    Assess the maturity level for a governance domain based on yes/no
    responses to assessment questions.

    Mapping:
      0 'yes' -> INITIAL
      1 'yes' -> DEVELOPING
      2-3 'yes' -> DEFINED
      4 'yes' -> MANAGED
      5 'yes' -> OPTIMIZING
    """
    yes_count = sum(responses)
    if yes_count == 0:
        return MaturityLevel.INITIAL
    elif yes_count == 1:
        return MaturityLevel.DEVELOPING
    elif yes_count <= 3:
        return MaturityLevel.DEFINED
    elif yes_count == 4:
        return MaturityLevel.MANAGED
    else:
        return MaturityLevel.OPTIMIZING


def compute_overall_maturity(
    domains: list[GovernanceDomain],
    levels: list[MaturityLevel],
) -> float:
    """
    Compute a weighted overall maturity score.

    Returns a float between 1.0 (all INITIAL) and 5.0 (all OPTIMIZING).
    """
    total_weight = sum(d.weight for d in domains)
    weighted_sum = sum(
        d.weight * int(level) for d, level in zip(domains, levels)
    )
    return round(weighted_sum / total_weight, 2)

## Discussion Questions

1. **The Asilomar analogy.** To what extent is the Asilomar Conference on recombinant DNA a useful model for OI governance? What aspects of OI make it fundamentally different from recombinant DNA, and how should governance frameworks account for those differences?

2. **Tiered regulation.** The chapter proposes a tiered regulatory framework (Table 21.3) in which oversight intensity scales with organoid complexity. What criteria should be used to classify organoid systems into tiers? Who should make these classification decisions, and how should disagreements be resolved?

3. **Regulatory arbitrage.** How can the international community prevent regulatory arbitrage in OI research — the migration of research to jurisdictions with the weakest oversight? Is a binding international treaty necessary, or can softer mechanisms (guidelines, norms, funding conditionality) achieve the same result?

4. **Self-governance vs. state regulation.** What is the appropriate balance between scientific self-governance and governmental regulation for OI? Under what conditions should the scientific community's own governance mechanisms be considered sufficient, and under what conditions is external regulation necessary?

5. **Democratic participation.** How can non-expert citizens meaningfully participate in OI governance decisions that require deep technical knowledge? What institutional mechanisms best combine scientific expertise with democratic legitimacy?

6. **Global South.** Most OI research is currently conducted in wealthy countries. How can governance frameworks ensure that the benefits of OI technology are equitably shared with developing countries, and that developing countries have a meaningful voice in governance decisions?

7. **Enforcement.** What enforcement mechanisms are most likely to be effective for OI governance, given that the technology is relatively low-cost, geographically distributed, and difficult to monitor? How can governance frameworks be designed to be enforceable across diverse jurisdictions?

8. **Precaution vs. innovation.** How should OI governance balance the precautionary principle (restricting activities until risks are well understood) with the innovation imperative (allowing research to proceed to realize potential benefits)? Where should the burden of proof lie — with those who want to proceed or those who want to restrict?

## Future Directions

### 🔮 Open Problems

1. **The measurement problem for tiered governance.** Tiered regulation (Table 21.3) requires objective, measurable criteria for classifying organoid systems by complexity and capability. What biomarkers, electrophysiological signatures, or behavioral tests should be used to assign organoids to regulatory tiers, and how can these criteria be validated and standardized internationally?

2. **Governance of emergent properties.** OI systems may exhibit emergent properties — capabilities that arise from the interaction of components and are not predictable from the properties of those components alone. How should governance frameworks account for the possibility that an organoid system classified at one tier may develop capabilities characteristic of a higher tier during the course of research?

3. **Enforcement in the absence of material controls.** Unlike nuclear technology (where fissile material can be tracked) or pharmaceutical manufacturing (where facilities can be inspected), OI research requires relatively modest infrastructure and widely available biological materials. How can governance frameworks be designed to be enforceable when the technology is inherently difficult to monitor and control?

4. **Integrating diverse legal and moral traditions.** International OI governance must accommodate legal traditions (common law, civil law, Islamic law, Confucian-influenced systems, Indigenous legal traditions) that embody fundamentally different assumptions about personhood, property, consent, and the moral status of non-human entities. How can a governance framework be sufficiently flexible to accommodate this diversity while maintaining meaningful minimum standards?

5. **Governing OI in the context of converging technologies.** OI does not exist in isolation; it converges with AI, brain-computer interfaces, synthetic biology, and nanotechnology. How should governance frameworks address the interactions between these converging technologies, including compound risks and emergent governance challenges that arise from their combination?

## Chapter Summary

This chapter surveyed the emerging global governance landscape for organoid intelligence, drawing on historical precedents, current regulatory frameworks, and proposed governance models. We began with the Asilomar Conference of 1975 — the paradigmatic example of scientific self-governance for a transformative biotechnology — and extracted lessons about timing, participation, international coordination, and adaptability that are directly relevant to OI governance. We then examined two additional historical precedents — nuclear governance through the IAEA and ongoing efforts to govern artificial intelligence — identifying both models to emulate and mistakes to avoid.

The current regulatory landscape (Section 21.2) is fragmented and incomplete. While existing frameworks — the ISSCR guidelines, the 14-day rule, FDA and EMA regulatory pathways, and national legislation in the US, EU, UK, Japan, China, and elsewhere — provide some governance infrastructure, none was designed with organoid intelligence in mind. Critical gaps remain (Section 21.3): the classification problem (organoids do not fit neatly into existing regulatory categories), dual-use concerns, the risk of regulatory arbitrage, and the persistent speed gap between technological innovation and regulatory development.

We examined proposed governance frameworks (Section 21.4) — adaptive governance, tiered regulation, institutional oversight, scientific self-governance, and multi-stakeholder models — and argued that effective OI governance will require elements of all these approaches, integrated into a coherent architecture. International coordination (Section 21.5) is essential but challenging, requiring new institutional arrangements or the adaptation of existing international organizations. We explored the potential roles of UNESCO, WHO, the OECD, and other international bodies, and sketched model treaty provisions for an eventual international OI governance framework. Institutional design for OI oversight (Section 21.6) must address national ethics committees, research ethics board adaptations, industry self-regulation, public engagement, and enforcement mechanisms. Finally, responsible innovation and anticipatory governance (Section 21.7) provide the overarching intellectual framework for building governance capacity ahead of technological capability — the central lesson of every historical precedent examined in this chapter.
