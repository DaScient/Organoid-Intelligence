This glossary provides definitions for key terms used throughout the textbook. Terms are organized alphabetically, and cross-references indicate the chapter(s) where each concept is discussed in depth.

---

## A

**Action potential** — A rapid, transient reversal of the electrical potential across a neuron's membrane, propagating along the axon as the fundamental unit of neural signaling. Action potentials follow an all-or-nothing principle governed by voltage-gated ion channels. *(Chapters 3, 7)*

**Active inference** — A corollary of the free energy principle in which an organism (or organoid) minimizes surprise by updating both its internal model (perception) and acting on the environment to confirm predictions. *(Chapter 11)*

**Adeno-associated virus (AAV)** — A small, non-pathogenic virus widely used as a vector for delivering genetic material—such as optogenetic constructs—into target cells with high efficiency and low immunogenicity. *(Chapters 5, 9)*

**Aggregate culture** — A three-dimensional cell culture method in which dissociated cells are allowed to self-assemble into spherical clusters, often used as a precursor step in organoid generation protocols.

**Agonist** — A molecule that binds to a receptor and activates it, mimicking the action of a natural ligand; used in pharmacological studies of organoid neural circuits. *(Chapter 16)*

**Angiogenesis** — The formation of new blood vessels from pre-existing vasculature, a critical challenge in organoid engineering for ensuring nutrient and oxygen delivery to deep tissue layers. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**Antagonist** — A molecule that binds to a receptor without activating it, thereby blocking the receptor's natural ligand; commonly used in drug screening with organoid models. *(Chapter 16)*

**Apical-basal polarity** — The asymmetric organization of a cell or tissue along an axis running from the apical (outer/luminal) surface to the basal (inner/matrix-attached) surface, essential for proper neural tube and organoid architecture. *(Chapter 4)*

**Assembloid** — A composite three-dimensional neural structure formed by fusing two or more region-specific organoids, enabling the study of inter-regional connectivity, circuit formation, and cell migration. *(Chapters 4, 6, 14)*

**Astrocyte** — A star-shaped glial cell that supports neurons by regulating the extracellular ionic environment, modulating synaptic transmission, maintaining the blood-brain barrier, and providing metabolic support. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**Autocrine signaling** — A form of cell communication in which a cell secretes a signaling molecule that binds to receptors on its own surface, relevant to organoid self-organization and differentiation.

**Axon** — The long, slender projection of a neuron that conducts action potentials away from the cell body toward synaptic terminals, enabling long-range neural communication. *(Chapter 3)*

## B

**Batch variability** — The inherent variation in organoid size, morphology, and cell composition across independent production runs, representing a major challenge for reproducibility and standardized biocomputation. *(Chapter 13)*

**Bayesian inference** — A statistical framework for updating the probability of a hypothesis as new evidence is acquired, providing a mathematical foundation for models of neural computation and predictive coding. *(Chapters 11, 12)*

**BCM theory** — The Bienenstock–Cooper–Munro theory of synaptic modification, which proposes a sliding threshold for long-term potentiation versus depression depending on the postsynaptic neuron's recent activity history. *(Chapter 3)*

**Biocompatibility** — The property of a material or device to interface with living biological tissue without eliciting a harmful immune response, degradation, or toxicity; essential for neural interface design. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Bioethics** — The study of ethical questions arising from advances in biology and medicine, including debates over organoid moral status, consent, and the boundaries of creating sentient biological systems. *(Chapters 19, 20)*

**Biofabrication** — The production of complex biological structures using a combination of living cells, biomaterials, and fabrication techniques such as bioprinting, aimed at engineering functional tissues and organs. *(Chapter 13)*

**Bioprinting** — An additive manufacturing technology that deposits bioinks—mixtures of living cells and supportive biomaterials—layer by layer to construct three-dimensional tissue architectures with precise spatial control. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**Bioreactor** — An engineered vessel or system that provides a controlled environment—including temperature, pH, oxygen, and nutrient supply—for maintaining and maturing organoid cultures at scale. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**Blood-brain barrier (BBB)** — A highly selective semipermeable boundary formed by endothelial cells lining brain capillaries, restricting the passage of substances from the bloodstream into the brain; recapitulated in advanced organoid models for drug testing. *(Chapters 4, 16)*

**Brain organoid** — A three-dimensional, self-organized neural tissue derived from pluripotent stem cells that recapitulates aspects of human brain development, architecture, and function in vitro. *(Chapters 1, 2, 4)*

**Brainoware** — An experimental bio-digital hybrid system developed by researchers at Indiana University that uses brain organoids coupled with multi-electrode arrays to perform computational tasks such as speech recognition. *(Chapters 1, 15, 17)*

## C

**Calcium imaging** — A fluorescence-based technique that uses calcium-sensitive indicators to visualize intracellular calcium transients, serving as a proxy for neural activity across large populations of cells simultaneously. *(Chapters 7, 9)*

**Cell fate** — The developmental path a cell follows toward a specific differentiated identity, determined by a combination of intrinsic gene regulatory networks and extrinsic signaling cues. *(Chapter 4)*

**Cerebral organoid** — A specific type of brain organoid that models aspects of cerebral cortex development, originally described by Lancaster et al. (2013), exhibiting self-organized cortical layering and regional heterogeneity. *(Chapters 2, 4)*

**Channelrhodopsin** — A light-sensitive ion channel protein derived from algae that depolarizes neurons upon illumination with blue light, widely used in optogenetic experiments to activate specific neural populations. *(Chapter 9)*

**Closed-loop stimulation** — A neurostimulation paradigm in which neural activity is recorded in real time and used to adaptively adjust stimulus parameters, enabling dynamic feedback between the organoid and external systems. *(Chapters 7, 15)*

**CMOS MEA** — A complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor multi-electrode array, a high-density recording platform fabricated using semiconductor technology that enables simultaneous electrophysiological recording from thousands of electrodes at single-cell resolution. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Computational complexity** — A measure of the resources (time, space, energy) required to solve a computational problem, relevant to assessing the theoretical power and efficiency of organoid-based biocomputers. *(Chapter 10)*

**Conduction velocity** — The speed at which an action potential propagates along an axon, determined by axon diameter, myelination status, and ion channel density; a key metric of neural circuit maturation. *(Chapters 3, 7)*

**Confocal microscopy** — An optical imaging technique that uses point illumination and spatial filtering to produce high-resolution optical sections of thick specimens such as organoids, enabling three-dimensional structural analysis.

**Cortical organoid** — A brain organoid specifically directed to develop into tissue resembling the cerebral cortex through the application of patterning factors that promote dorsal forebrain identity. *(Chapter 4)*

**CRISPR-Cas9** — A genome-editing technology that enables precise modification of DNA sequences in living cells, used in organoid research to create disease models, insert reporters, or introduce optogenetic constructs. *(Chapters 5, 16)*

**Cross-frequency coupling** — A phenomenon in which the phase or amplitude of one neural oscillation frequency modulates the amplitude or phase of another, thought to coordinate information processing across spatial and temporal scales. *(Chapter 12)*

**Cytoarchitecture** — The spatial arrangement and organization of cell types within a tissue, including cortical layering patterns that emerge during organoid maturation and serve as markers of developmental fidelity. *(Chapter 4)*

---

## D

**Deep brain organoid** — An organoid engineered to model subcortical brain structures such as the thalamus, hypothalamus, or basal ganglia, enabling the study of deep brain circuits and their contributions to behavior and computation. *(Chapters 4, 6)*

**Dendrite** — A branched extension of a neuron that receives synaptic inputs from other neurons and conducts graded electrical signals toward the cell body, integrating information from multiple sources. *(Chapter 3)*

**Differentiation** — The process by which a less specialized cell becomes a more specialized cell type with distinct morphology, gene expression, and function; the central process in organoid generation. *(Chapter 4)*

**DishBrain** — A landmark experiment by Cortical Labs (2022) in which human and mouse cortical neurons cultured on multi-electrode arrays learned to play a simplified version of the video game Pong through closed-loop electrophysiological feedback. *(Chapters 1, 11, 17)*

**Dorsal-ventral axis** — The anatomical axis running from the back (dorsal) to the belly (ventral) side of the developing nervous system, along which distinct neural progenitor domains are specified by opposing morphogen gradients. *(Chapter 4)*

**Dual SMAD inhibition** — A widely used neural induction protocol that simultaneously inhibits BMP and TGF-β/Activin/Nodal signaling pathways using small molecules (e.g., LDN193189 and SB431542), efficiently converting pluripotent stem cells into neuroectodermal progenitors. *(Chapters 4, 5)*

---

## E

**Echo state network** — A type of recurrent neural network within the reservoir computing framework in which the recurrent layer (reservoir) has fixed, randomly initialized weights, and only the output (readout) weights are trained. *(Chapter 10)*

**Efferent neuron** — A neuron that carries signals away from the central nervous system toward effector organs such as muscles or glands; also called a motor neuron. *(Chapter 3)*

**Electrical stimulation** — The application of controlled electrical current or voltage to neural tissue via electrodes to evoke, modulate, or suppress neural activity in organoid biocomputing systems. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Electroporation** — A technique that uses brief, high-voltage electrical pulses to transiently permeabilize cell membranes, enabling the introduction of DNA, RNA, or proteins into cells, including those within organoids. *(Chapter 5)*

**Embryoid body (EB)** — A three-dimensional aggregate of pluripotent stem cells that spontaneously differentiates into cells of all three germ layers, often used as an intermediate step in organoid generation protocols. *(Chapter 4)*

**Emergent property** — A complex system-level behavior or characteristic that arises from the interactions of simpler components but is not predictable from the properties of those components alone; central to organoid intelligence. *(Chapters 1, 10)*

**Engram** — A hypothetical physical trace or substrate of memory stored in the brain, corresponding to lasting changes in synaptic connectivity and neural circuit structure. *(Chapter 3)*

**Entropy** — In information theory, a measure of the uncertainty or unpredictability in a random variable; in thermodynamics, a measure of disorder; both interpretations are relevant to neural coding analyses. *(Chapter 12)*

**Epigenetic landscape** — A metaphorical model proposed by C.H. Waddington depicting cell differentiation as a ball rolling down a landscape of branching valleys, representing the progressive restriction of cell fate potential. *(Chapter 4)*

**Excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP)** — A graded depolarization of the postsynaptic neuron's membrane potential caused by the binding of excitatory neurotransmitters, bringing the cell closer to the threshold for firing an action potential. *(Chapter 3)*

**Explainability** — The degree to which the internal workings and decision-making processes of a computational system—including organoid-based biocomputers—can be understood and interpreted by humans. *(Chapters 17, 21)*

**Extracellular matrix (ECM)** — A complex network of proteins and polysaccharides secreted by cells that provides structural support, biochemical signaling cues, and a scaffold for tissue organization; Matrigel is a commonly used ECM substitute. *(Chapters 4, 8)*

---

## F

**Fading memory property** — The characteristic of a dynamical system (such as a recurrent neural network or organoid) in which the system's current state is influenced more strongly by recent inputs than by distant past inputs, essential for reservoir computing. *(Chapter 10)*

**Fiber photometry** — An optical recording technique that uses implanted optical fibers to measure bulk fluorescence signals—typically from genetically encoded calcium or neurotransmitter indicators—in defined brain regions or organoid volumes. *(Chapter 9)*

**Firing rate** — The number of action potentials a neuron generates per unit time, a primary variable in rate-coding models of neural information processing. *(Chapters 3, 12)*

**Fluorescence microscopy** — A microscopy technique that uses fluorescent labels to visualize specific molecules, structures, or cell types within organoids, enabling analysis of protein expression, cell identity, and neural activity.

**Forebrain organoid** — An organoid patterned to develop characteristics of the forebrain (prosencephalon), including cortical, hippocampal, and striatal identities, through directed differentiation with specific morphogen combinations. *(Chapter 4)*

**Free energy principle** — A unifying theory proposed by Karl Friston stating that all adaptive biological systems act to minimize variational free energy—a bound on surprise—providing a mathematical framework for perception, action, and learning. *(Chapter 11)*

**Functional connectivity** — A measure of the statistical dependence or correlation between the activity patterns of distinct neural populations, inferred from electrophysiological or imaging data without implying direct anatomical connections. *(Chapters 7, 12, 14)*

---

## G

**GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)** — The principal inhibitory neurotransmitter in the mature mammalian brain, synthesized by interneurons; GABA signaling is critical for maintaining excitatory-inhibitory balance in organoid neural networks. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**Generative model** — An internal model maintained by a neural system that generates predictions about expected sensory inputs; discrepancies between predictions and actual inputs (prediction errors) drive learning and inference. *(Chapter 11)*

**Gene regulatory network (GRN)** — A set of interacting genes and their regulatory elements that control gene expression patterns during development, differentiation, and cellular response to environmental signals in organoids. *(Chapter 4)*

**Glial cells** — Non-neuronal cells of the nervous system—including astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and microglia—that provide structural support, metabolic regulation, myelination, and immune defense; increasingly recognized as contributors to neural computation. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**Glutamate** — The most abundant excitatory neurotransmitter in the vertebrate nervous system, released at the majority of excitatory synapses and essential for processes such as synaptic plasticity and long-term potentiation. *(Chapter 3)*

**GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice)** — A set of regulatory standards and quality assurance protocols governing the production of pharmaceuticals, biologics, and cell-based products, applicable to the manufacture of clinical-grade organoids. *(Chapter 13)*

**Graded potential** — A variable-amplitude change in membrane potential that diminishes with distance from its point of origin, contrasting with the all-or-nothing nature of action potentials; includes EPSPs and IPSPs. *(Chapter 3)*

**Growth factors** — Naturally occurring proteins that stimulate cell proliferation, differentiation, and survival by binding to specific receptors; key examples in organoid culture include EGF, FGF, and BDNF. *(Chapters 4, 5)*

**Guided differentiation** — A protocol-driven approach to organoid generation that uses defined combinations of growth factors, small molecules, and morphogens to direct stem cell differentiation toward specific brain region identities. *(Chapter 4)*

---

## H

**Halorhodopsin** — A light-driven chloride pump derived from archaeal species that hyperpolarizes neurons upon illumination with yellow light, used in optogenetics to silence or inhibit specific neural populations. *(Chapter 9)*

**Hebbian learning** — A synaptic plasticity rule often summarized as "neurons that fire together wire together," postulating that simultaneous activation of pre- and postsynaptic neurons strengthens their shared synaptic connection. *(Chapters 3, 10)*

**High-throughput screening** — An automated experimental approach that rapidly tests large numbers of compounds, genetic perturbations, or conditions on organoid cultures in parallel, accelerating drug discovery and functional genomics. *(Chapters 13, 16)*

**Hindbrain organoid** — An organoid patterned to develop features characteristic of the hindbrain (rhombencephalon), including cerebellar and brainstem identities, through directed differentiation protocols. *(Chapter 4)*

**Hodgkin-Huxley model** — A mathematical model describing the ionic mechanisms underlying the initiation and propagation of action potentials, based on voltage-clamp experiments in the squid giant axon; foundational to computational neuroscience. *(Chapters 3, 12)*

**Homeostatic plasticity** — A set of negative-feedback mechanisms that stabilize neural circuit activity within a functional range by adjusting synaptic strengths, intrinsic excitability, or neurotransmitter release probabilities. *(Chapter 3)*

**Hybrid bio-digital system** — An integrated computational platform that couples biological neural tissue (such as organoids) with conventional digital electronics to leverage the complementary strengths of both substrates. *(Chapters 15, 17)*

**Hydrogel** — A water-swollen polymer network used as a scaffold or embedding material in organoid culture, providing mechanical support and a biochemically tunable microenvironment for three-dimensional tissue growth. *(Chapters 4, 8)*

---

## I

**Impedance** — The total opposition to alternating current flow in a circuit, comprising resistance and reactance; a critical parameter in electrode design for neural interfaces that affects signal quality. *(Chapter 7)*

**Induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)** — A type of pluripotent stem cell generated by reprogramming adult somatic cells (e.g., skin fibroblasts) through the expression of specific transcription factors (Yamanaka factors), capable of differentiating into virtually any cell type. *(Chapters 2, 4, 5)*

**Information theory** — A mathematical framework founded by Claude Shannon for quantifying information content, transmission capacity, and coding efficiency; applied to neural spike train analysis and organoid computation. *(Chapter 12)*

**Inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP)** — A graded hyperpolarization of the postsynaptic membrane potential caused by the binding of inhibitory neurotransmitters such as GABA, decreasing the probability of action potential generation. *(Chapter 3)*

**Interneuron** — A neuron that forms connections exclusively within a local circuit, mediating communication between sensory and motor neurons and playing a critical role in shaping network dynamics through inhibition. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**In vitro** — A Latin term meaning "in glass," referring to experiments or processes conducted outside a living organism in a controlled laboratory environment, as with organoid culture systems.

**ISSCR (International Society for Stem Cell Research)** — A professional organization that issues guidelines and ethical frameworks for stem cell and organoid research, including recommendations on the 14-day rule and organoid complexity limits. *(Chapters 19, 20)*

---

## J

**Junction potential** — The voltage difference that arises at the interface between two different electrolyte solutions (e.g., between the electrode filling solution and the extracellular medium), which must be corrected in electrophysiological recordings. *(Chapter 7)*

---

## K

**Kirchhoff's current law** — A fundamental principle of circuit analysis stating that the sum of currents entering a node equals the sum of currents leaving it; applied in modeling ionic current flow through neuronal membranes and in multi-electrode array circuit design. *(Chapter 7)*

---

## L

**Lancaster protocol** — A seminal method developed by Madeline Lancaster and colleagues for generating cerebral organoids from pluripotent stem cells using an unguided, self-organizing approach embedded in Matrigel droplets. *(Chapters 2, 4)*

**Lateral inhibition** — A neural circuit motif in which an excited neuron suppresses the activity of its neighbors, enhancing spatial contrast and contributing to pattern formation during development and sharpening of neural representations. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**Lineage tracing** — A technique used to track the developmental origin and fate of individual cells and their progeny over time, employing genetic reporters or barcoding to map clonal relationships within organoids. *(Chapter 4)*

**Liquid state machine (LSM)** — A computational model within the reservoir computing paradigm that uses a recurrent network of spiking neurons as a high-dimensional, dynamic reservoir for temporal signal processing. *(Chapter 10)*

**Local field potential (LFP)** — The low-frequency component of the extracellular electrical signal recorded near neural tissue, reflecting the summed synaptic currents and subthreshold membrane oscillations of a local population of neurons. *(Chapters 7, 12)*

**Long-term depression (LTD)** — A persistent weakening of synaptic strength following specific patterns of stimulation, complementary to LTP and considered a cellular mechanism for forgetting and synaptic refinement. *(Chapter 3)*

**Long-term potentiation (LTP)** — A persistent strengthening of synaptic connections following high-frequency or coincident stimulation, widely regarded as a cellular substrate of learning and memory. *(Chapters 3, 10)*

---

## M

**Machine learning** — A branch of artificial intelligence in which computational algorithms improve their performance on a task through experience (data), used to decode organoid neural activity and optimize biocomputer operation. *(Chapters 15, 17)*

**Matrigel** — A commercially available basement membrane extract derived from Engelbreth-Holm-Swarm mouse sarcoma cells, widely used as an extracellular matrix scaffold for embedding and supporting organoid growth. *(Chapters 4, 5)*

**MEA (multi-electrode array)** — A device consisting of multiple microelectrodes arranged on a substrate for simultaneously recording and stimulating electrical activity from many sites across a neural culture or organoid. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Microfluidics** — A technology involving the precise manipulation of fluids at the micrometer scale through engineered channels and chambers, used to control the chemical microenvironment of organoids and deliver nutrients or signaling molecules. *(Chapters 8, 13)*

**Midbrain organoid** — An organoid directed to develop features of the midbrain (mesencephalon), including dopaminergic neurons, used for modeling Parkinson's disease and studying midbrain-specific circuitry. *(Chapters 4, 16)*

**Morphogen** — A signaling molecule that forms a concentration gradient across developing tissue, specifying distinct cell fates at different threshold concentrations; examples include SHH, BMP, and WNT. *(Chapter 4)*

**Motor neuron** — A neuron whose cell body resides in the central nervous system and whose axon projects to muscles or glands to control movement and secretion. *(Chapter 3)*

**Multiplexing** — The simultaneous recording or stimulation of multiple independent signals through a shared set of channels or electrodes, critical for scaling organoid neural interfaces. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Mutual information** — An information-theoretic measure quantifying the amount of information that one random variable contains about another, used to assess the statistical dependence between stimulus and neural response in organoid recordings. *(Chapter 12)*

**Myelination** — The process by which oligodendrocytes (in the CNS) or Schwann cells (in the PNS) wrap axons in insulating myelin sheaths, dramatically increasing conduction velocity and metabolic efficiency. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

---

## N

**Necrotic core** — A region of cell death at the center of large organoids caused by inadequate diffusion of oxygen and nutrients, representing a major engineering challenge addressed by vascularization strategies and bioreactor design. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**Neural coding** — The study of how information about stimuli, decisions, and motor commands is represented in patterns of neural activity, including rate, temporal, population, and phase coding schemes. *(Chapter 12)*

**Neural crest cell** — A migratory cell population arising from the dorsal neural tube that gives rise to diverse derivatives including peripheral neurons, glia, and craniofacial structures.

**Neural dust** — Ultraminiaturized, wireless neural recording devices (on the order of millimeters or smaller) designed for minimally invasive, long-term monitoring of neural activity, with potential applications in organoid interfaces. *(Chapter 8)*

**Neural induction** — The developmental process by which ectodermal cells are directed toward a neural fate, typically through the inhibition of BMP and TGF-β signaling; replicated in vitro using dual SMAD inhibition protocols. *(Chapter 4)*

**Neural network (artificial)** — A computational model inspired by biological neural networks, consisting of interconnected processing nodes (artificial neurons) organized in layers, used for pattern recognition, classification, and prediction. *(Chapters 10, 17)*

**Neural progenitor cell (NPC)** — A multipotent cell in the developing nervous system capable of self-renewal and differentiation into the major neural cell types: neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes. *(Chapters 4, 5)*

**Neural rosette** — A radially organized structure of neural progenitor cells in two-dimensional culture that recapitulates the architecture of the developing neural tube, serving as an early indicator of successful neural induction. *(Chapter 4)*

**Neurodegeneration** — The progressive loss of structure and function of neurons, characteristic of diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS; organoid models enable patient-specific disease modeling and drug screening. *(Chapter 16)*

**Neuroplasticity** — The capacity of the nervous system to modify its structure and function in response to experience, injury, or changing environmental demands, encompassing synaptic, structural, and homeostatic forms of plasticity. *(Chapters 3, 10)*

**Neurosphere** — A free-floating, spherical aggregate of neural stem and progenitor cells grown in suspension culture, used as an assay for neural stem cell self-renewal and multipotency. *(Chapter 4)*

**Neurotransmitter** — A chemical messenger released from the presynaptic terminal of a neuron that crosses the synaptic cleft and binds to receptors on the postsynaptic cell to transmit signals. *(Chapter 3)*

**Node of Ranvier** — A periodic gap in the myelin sheath along a myelinated axon where voltage-gated ion channels are concentrated, enabling saltatory conduction and rapid signal propagation. *(Chapter 3)*

**Noise entropy** — A component of total entropy that measures the variability of neural responses to repeated presentations of the same stimulus, reflecting the intrinsic stochasticity of neural firing. *(Chapter 12)*

---

## O

**Oligodendrocyte** — A glial cell of the central nervous system responsible for producing myelin sheaths that insulate axons, increasing conduction velocity; their presence in organoids is a marker of advanced maturation. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

**Opsins** — A family of light-sensitive proteins used in optogenetics that function as ion channels or pumps, enabling optical control of neural activity with high temporal and cell-type specificity. *(Chapter 9)*

**Optogenetics** — A technique combining genetic engineering and optics that uses light-sensitive proteins (opsins) expressed in specific cell populations to precisely control neural activity with millisecond temporal resolution. *(Chapters 9, 15)*

**Organoid** — A three-dimensional, self-organized tissue structure derived from stem cells that recapitulates key aspects of organ architecture, cellular diversity, and function in vitro. *(Chapters 1, 2, 4)*

**Organoid intelligence (OI)** — An emerging interdisciplinary field that harnesses the computational properties of brain organoids—including self-organization, synaptic plasticity, and adaptive learning—for information processing, pattern recognition, and bio-digital hybrid computing. *(Chapters 1, 2, 17, 22)*

**Oscillation (neural)** — Rhythmic, repetitive patterns of neural activity at characteristic frequencies (e.g., theta, alpha, gamma), believed to coordinate information processing, communication between brain regions, and cognitive functions. *(Chapters 3, 12)*

---

## P

**Paracrine signaling** — A form of cell-to-cell communication in which a cell secretes signaling molecules that act on nearby target cells, mediating local coordination of differentiation and patterning within organoids. *(Chapter 4)*

**Passage** — The process of enzymatically dissociating and re-plating cultured cells to maintain their proliferative capacity and prevent overcrowding, a routine step in stem cell and organoid maintenance.

**Patch clamp** — A high-resolution electrophysiological technique that uses a glass micropipette to form a tight seal with a cell membrane, enabling the recording of ionic currents through individual ion channels or whole-cell activity. *(Chapter 7)*

**Phase coding** — A neural coding scheme in which information is represented by the timing of a neuron's spikes relative to the phase of an ongoing oscillation, enabling multiplexed information transmission. *(Chapter 12)*

**Pluripotency** — The capacity of a stem cell to differentiate into any cell type derived from the three embryonic germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) but not into extraembryonic tissues. *(Chapter 4)*

**Population coding** — A representational scheme in which information is encoded by the joint activity patterns of a group of neurons rather than by the activity of individual cells. *(Chapter 12)*

**Predictive processing** — A theoretical framework proposing that the brain continuously generates and updates predictions about incoming sensory inputs, with learning driven by the minimization of prediction errors. *(Chapter 11)*

**Pyramidal neuron** — The principal excitatory neuron of the cerebral cortex, characterized by a pyramid-shaped soma, a single apical dendrite, and multiple basal dendrites; the predominant cell type in cortical organoids. *(Chapters 3, 4)*

---

## Q

**Quality control (QC)** — A set of standardized assays, metrics, and procedures used to evaluate organoid identity, purity, viability, maturation, and functional performance, ensuring consistency and reliability for biocomputation applications. *(Chapter 13)*

**Quiescence** — A reversible state of cell cycle arrest in which cells remain viable but non-proliferative, relevant to understanding neural stem cell behavior within organoids.

**Quorum sensing** — A cell-to-cell communication mechanism, originally described in bacterial populations, in which signaling molecule concentration scales with cell density to coordinate collective behavior; analogous mechanisms may operate in organoid self-organization. *(Chapter 14)*

---

## R

**Rate coding** — A neural coding scheme in which information about a stimulus is represented by the average firing rate of a neuron over a defined time window. *(Chapter 12)*

**Readout layer** — In reservoir computing, the output component that maps the high-dimensional internal states of the reservoir (e.g., an organoid neural network) to desired outputs through trained linear weights. *(Chapter 10)*

**Reprogramming** — The process of converting a differentiated somatic cell back to a pluripotent state, typically achieved through the forced expression of Yamanaka factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc). *(Chapters 2, 4)*

**Reservoir computing** — A computational framework in which a fixed, high-dimensional dynamical system (the reservoir) transforms input signals into a rich set of internal states from which a simple readout layer extracts desired outputs. *(Chapter 10)*

**Resting membrane potential** — The steady-state voltage difference across a neuron's membrane in the absence of stimulation, typically around −70 mV, maintained by the differential distribution of ions and the selective permeability of ion channels. *(Chapter 3)*

---

## S

**Saltatory conduction** — The discontinuous propagation of action potentials along a myelinated axon, in which the impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier, resulting in faster and more energy-efficient signal transmission. *(Chapter 3)*

**Scaffold** — A three-dimensional structural support—natural (e.g., Matrigel, decellularized ECM) or synthetic (e.g., PLGA, hydrogel)—that provides a framework for cell attachment, migration, and tissue organization in organoid engineering. *(Chapters 4, 8)*

**Self-organization** — The spontaneous emergence of ordered spatial and temporal patterns in a system through local interactions among its components, without external direction; a hallmark of organoid development. *(Chapters 1, 4)*

**Shannon entropy** — A measure of the average information content or uncertainty in a discrete random variable, defined as H(X) = −Σ p(x) log₂ p(x); used to quantify the information capacity of neural spike trains. *(Chapter 12)*

**Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)** — The ratio of the power of a desired signal to the power of background noise, a critical metric for evaluating the quality of electrophysiological recordings from organoid interfaces. *(Chapters 7, 8)*

**Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)** — A high-throughput genomic technique that profiles gene expression in individual cells, enabling the identification and characterization of distinct cell types and developmental states within organoids. *(Chapters 4, 5)*

**Small molecule** — A low molecular weight organic compound that can modulate biological processes by interacting with specific protein targets; widely used in directed differentiation protocols and drug screening. *(Chapters 4, 16)*

**Spike sorting** — A computational process for identifying and classifying individual neuronal action potentials (spikes) from extracellular multi-electrode recordings based on waveform shape, amplitude, and temporal characteristics. *(Chapter 7)*

**Spike train** — A sequence of action potentials (spikes) produced by a neuron over time, typically represented as a series of binary events; the fundamental data type for neural coding analyses. *(Chapters 7, 12)*

**STDP (spike-timing-dependent plasticity)** — A form of Hebbian synaptic plasticity in which the direction and magnitude of synaptic weight changes depend on the precise temporal order and interval between pre- and postsynaptic spikes. *(Chapters 3, 10)*

**Stem cell** — An undifferentiated cell capable of self-renewal and differentiation into specialized cell types; embryonic stem cells (ESCs) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) are the primary sources for organoid generation. *(Chapters 2, 4)*

**Subventricular zone (SVZ)** — A germinal region adjacent to the ventricles in the developing brain where neural progenitor cells proliferate and generate new neurons; recapitulated in brain organoids as a marker of cortical development. *(Chapter 4)*

**Synapse** — A specialized junction between two neurons through which signals are transmitted, either chemically (via neurotransmitter release) or electrically (via gap junctions). *(Chapter 3)*

**Synaptic plasticity** — The ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time in response to changes in activity patterns, encompassing mechanisms such as LTP, LTD, and STDP; the cellular basis of learning and adaptation. *(Chapters 3, 10, 11)*

**Synaptogenesis** — The formation of new synaptic connections between neurons during development, a process that continues during organoid maturation and is essential for establishing functional neural circuits. *(Chapter 4)*

---

## T

**Temporal coding** — A neural coding scheme in which information is carried by the precise timing of individual spikes or spike patterns rather than by average firing rates. *(Chapter 12)*

**Tissue clearing** — A set of chemical and optical techniques that render biological tissues transparent by removing lipids and matching refractive indices, enabling volumetric imaging of whole organoids with light-sheet or confocal microscopy.

**Transcription factor** — A protein that binds to specific DNA sequences to regulate the transcription of target genes, controlling cell fate decisions during development and reprogramming (e.g., the Yamanaka factors). *(Chapter 4)*

**Transcriptomics** — The comprehensive study of the complete set of RNA transcripts (the transcriptome) produced by a cell, tissue, or organoid under specific conditions, often using RNA sequencing approaches. *(Chapter 5)*

**Transfer entropy** — A model-free, information-theoretic measure of the directed (time-asymmetric) transfer of information between two time series, used to infer causal or directed connectivity in organoid neural networks. *(Chapter 12)*

**Turing completeness** — The property of a computational system that can simulate any Turing machine, meaning it is capable of universal computation given sufficient time and memory; a theoretical benchmark for evaluating organoid computing potential. *(Chapter 10)*

---

## U

**Undirected differentiation** — An organoid generation approach in which pluripotent stem cells are allowed to self-organize and differentiate without the addition of exogenous patterning factors, resulting in heterogeneous tissue with multiple brain region identities. *(Chapter 4)*

---

## V

**Vascularization** — The process of forming blood vessels within an organoid, either through co-culture with endothelial cells, transplantation into host tissue, or bioengineering approaches; critical for overcoming diffusion-limited oxygen and nutrient delivery. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor)** — A signaling protein that promotes angiogenesis and vasculogenesis by stimulating the proliferation and migration of endothelial cells; used in organoid vascularization strategies. *(Chapters 4, 13)*

**Ventricular zone (VZ)** — The innermost germinal layer of the developing brain lining the ventricles, containing radial glial progenitor cells that give rise to neurons via asymmetric division; recapitulated in cortical organoids. *(Chapter 4)*

**Voltage-gated ion channel** — A transmembrane protein that opens or closes in response to changes in membrane potential, mediating the rapid ion fluxes underlying action potentials and synaptic transmission. *(Chapter 3)*

---

## W

**Waddington landscape** — A conceptual model proposed by Conrad Hal Waddington (1957) depicting cellular differentiation as a ball rolling through a landscape of ridges and valleys, where valleys represent stable cell fates and ridges represent barriers between fates. *(Chapter 4)*

**Whole-cell recording** — A patch-clamp configuration in which the membrane beneath the pipette tip is ruptured, providing electrical access to the entire cell interior for recording total ionic currents or membrane potential. *(Chapter 7)*

---

## X

**Xenograft** — A tissue graft transplanted from one species into another; human brain organoids transplanted into rodent hosts represent a xenograft model used to study vascularization, maturation, and circuit integration. *(Chapters 4, 19)*

---

## Y

**Yamanaka factors** — The four transcription factors—Oct4 (POU5F1), Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc—whose ectopic expression in somatic cells induces reprogramming to a pluripotent state, as demonstrated by Shinya Yamanaka's Nobel Prize-winning work. *(Chapters 2, 4)*

---

## Z

**Zona pellucida** — Although primarily associated with oocyte biology, this term is occasionally referenced in developmental biology contexts to describe protective extracellular coatings; in organoid science, analogous encapsulation strategies protect organoids during handling and transplantation.
